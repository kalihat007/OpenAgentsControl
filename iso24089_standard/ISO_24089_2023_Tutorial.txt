SECTION 1.1: EXECUTIVE OVERVIEW
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

ISO 24089:2023 represents a landmark achievement in automotive standardization,
establishing the world's first comprehensive international standard dedicated
exclusively to software update engineering for road vehicles. This standard
addresses the fundamental transformation occurring in the automotive industry
as vehicles evolve from predominantly mechanical systems to software-defined
platforms capable of receiving over-the-air (OTA) updates throughout their
operational lifetime.

The development of ISO 24089:2023 was driven by several critical industry needs
that emerged as vehicles became increasingly connected, automated, and dependent
on software for core functionality. Traditional automotive development models
assumed that once a vehicle left the manufacturing facility, its configuration
remained largely static until service interventions occurred at authorized
workshops. This paradigm has been fundamentally disrupted by the advent of
OTA update capabilities, which enable manufacturers to modify vehicle software
remotely, continuously improving functionality, addressing safety concerns,
and responding to cybersecurity threats throughout the vehicle's operational
life.

The standard emerged from the recognition that existing quality management and
functional safety frameworks, while essential, did not adequately address the
unique challenges posed by software update engineering. ISO 26262 provides
comprehensive guidance for achieving functional safety in automotive systems,
but its scope is primarily focused on the initial development and validation
of safety-critical systems. ISO/SAE 21434 addresses cybersecurity engineering
throughout the vehicle lifecycle, but software update engineering represents
a distinct discipline requiring specialized processes, organizational
structures, and technical capabilities.

The importance of ISO 24089:2023 cannot be overstated in the context of
regulatory developments worldwide. The United Nations Economic Commission for
Europe (UNECE) Regulation No. 156 established mandatory requirements for
software update and software update management systems (SUMS) for vehicle
type approval in participating countries. ISO 24089:2023 provides the
technical foundation and detailed implementation guidance necessary for
organizations to demonstrate compliance with these regulatory requirements
while ensuring that software update activities maintain the highest standards
of safety and security.

The standard's applicability extends across the entire automotive supply chain.
Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) bear primary responsibility for
ensuring that vehicles can safely receive and process software updates, but
Tier-1 suppliers providing electronic control units (ECUs) must also ensure
their products support update capabilities in compliance with the standard.
Infrastructure providers managing the backend systems for OTA distribution,
third-party service providers, and even vehicle fleet operators must
understand and implement relevant portions of the standard.

From a technical perspective, ISO 24089:2023 establishes a four-level
hierarchical architecture for organizing software update engineering activities:

LEVEL 1 - ORGANIZATIONAL: Governance, continuous improvement, information
sharing, supporting processes, and organizational auditing ensure that
appropriate management systems are in place to support software update
engineering throughout the organization.

LEVEL 2 - PROJECT: Planning, tailoring, interoperability confirmation, and
integrity preservation provide the project-level framework for managing
individual software update initiatives.

LEVEL 3 - INFRASTRUCTURE: Cybersecurity risk management, vehicle configuration
information management, campaign communication, and package processing
functions constitute the technical infrastructure supporting software update
campaigns.

LEVEL 4 - VEHICLE AND VEHICLE SYSTEMS: Risk management, configuration
information handling, campaign communication, and package processing at the
vehicle level ensure safe and effective update operations.

This hierarchical structure reflects the standard's recognition that effective
software update engineering requires coordinated activities across multiple
organizational levels, from enterprise-wide governance processes down to
individual vehicle-level update operations. Each level has distinct objectives,
requirements, and work products, but all levels must work in concert to
achieve safe and effective software update engineering.

The standard's development involved extensive collaboration among automotive
OEMs, Tier-1 suppliers, regulatory authorities, and technical experts from
around the world. The result is a consensus-based document that reflects
current industry best practices while establishing minimum requirements that
all organizations must meet. The standard does not prescribe specific
technologies or solutions, recognizing that the automotive industry encompasses
diverse vehicle architectures, update mechanisms, and organizational structures.
Instead, it establishes outcome-based requirements that organizations can
satisfy through various technical and organizational approaches appropriate
to their specific contexts.

SECTION 1.2: SCOPE OF THE STANDARD
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Reference: page_005.png through page_008.png

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"This document specifies requirements and recommendations for software update
engineering. This document is applicable to the engineering of software updates
for road vehicles. This document is applicable to organizations that perform
software update engineering activities. This document is applicable to vehicle
systems, ECUs and infrastructure, including their software. This document is
applicable to software that can be updated."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

The scope statement establishes the fundamental boundaries and applicability
of ISO 24089:2023 across five distinct dimensions, each of which requires
careful interpretation to understand the standard's full implications for
automotive organizations.

APPLICABILITY DIMENSION 1 - ORGANIZATIONAL LEVEL:

The standard applies to "organizations that perform software update engineering
activities." This language deliberately avoids limiting applicability to any
specific type of organization. The term "organization" encompasses:

- Original Equipment Manufacturers (OEMs) who design, manufacture, and
  market vehicles and are responsible for the overall software update
  engineering capability of their products.

- Tier-1 suppliers who develop and supply vehicle systems and ECUs to OEMs
  and are responsible for ensuring their products can receive and process
  software updates in accordance with OEM requirements and the standard.

- Tier-2 and lower-tier suppliers who provide software components or
  subsystems to Tier-1 suppliers, and whose work products must support
  the overall software update engineering capabilities of the final system.

- Infrastructure providers who operate backend systems for software update
  distribution, including cloud platforms, OTA management systems, and
  associated services.

- Service providers including authorized workshops, dealership networks,
  and independent service facilities that perform software update operations
  on vehicles.

- Fleet operators who manage vehicle fleets and may coordinate software
  update campaigns across multiple vehicles under their control.

The phrase "perform software update engineering activities" defines the
activities that bring an organization within the standard's scope. Software
update engineering activities include:

- Planning software update projects and campaigns
- Developing software update packages
- Assembling metadata and compatibility information
- Creating and managing infrastructure for software update distribution
- Developing vehicle capabilities for receiving and processing updates
- Executing software update campaigns
- Performing verification and validation activities
- Managing documentation and records
- Conducting organizational audits

Organizations that merely consume software updates but do not engage in
engineering activities related to software updates may have limited direct
applicability of the standard. However, such organizations should understand
the standard's requirements to effectively interact with suppliers and
partners who do perform software update engineering activities.

APPLICABILITY DIMENSION 2 - VEHICLE SYSTEMS:

The standard applies to "vehicle systems," which the standard defines as
"functional group of one or more ECUs and attached hardware." This definition
encompasses virtually all modern vehicle systems, including:

- Powertrain control systems (engine control module, transmission control,
  electric motor controllers, battery management systems)
- Chassis systems (braking systems, steering systems, suspension control,
  tire pressure monitoring)
- Body systems (body control modules, lighting systems, climate control,
  access systems)
- Advanced driver assistance systems (ADAS) (adaptive cruise control,
  lane keeping assist, automatic emergency braking, parking assistance)
- Infotainment systems (navigation, audio systems, connectivity modules,
  display systems)
- Telematics systems (communication modules, GPS receivers, vehicle-to-
  everything (V2X) systems)
- Safety systems (airbag controllers, occupant detection, seat belt
  pretensioners)

The application to vehicle systems means that organizations developing any
of these systems must consider software update engineering requirements when
designing and validating their products. This represents a significant
extension of traditional automotive development processes, which often treated
software as a completed artifact at the time of vehicle production.

APPLICABILITY DIMENSION 3 - ELECTRONIC CONTROL UNITS (ECUs):

The standard applies to "ECUs," defined as "embedded device in a vehicle
whose software can be updated." This definition is intentionally broad,
encompassing:

- Traditional automotive ECUs with flash-based program memory capable of
  in-vehicle programming
- Advanced domain controllers and zone controllers that consolidate
  multiple vehicle functions
- High-performance computing platforms supporting autonomous driving
  and advanced infotainment features
- Communication modules and gateways that manage vehicle network traffic
- Sensor modules with embedded firmware that can be updated
- Actuator controllers with software-controlled behavior

The definition explicitly includes ECUs "whose software can be updated,"
which suggests that ECUs with software permanently programmed at manufacture
(such as some legacy systems with mask-programmed ROM) may fall outside
the standard's scope for those specific units. However, most modern vehicles
contain predominantly updateable ECUs, making the standard broadly applicable.

APPLICABILITY DIMENSION 4 - INFRASTRUCTURE:

The standard applies to "infrastructure," which is defined as "processes and
information systems managing any combination of software update operations,
software update campaigns, documentation and vehicle configuration information,
including both digital and manual activities." This comprehensive definition
encompasses:

- Cloud-based backend systems for OTA update management
- Vehicle communication platforms enabling connectivity
- Software build and packaging systems
- Vehicle configuration databases and management systems
- Campaign management and scheduling systems
- Monitoring and analytics platforms for update tracking
- Security infrastructure including key management and certificate systems
- Workshop tools and diagnostic equipment
- Manual processes and procedures supporting update activities
- Documentation systems and record management

The inclusion of "manual activities" within the infrastructure definition
reflects the standard's recognition that software update engineering involves
human processes alongside technical systems. Organizations cannot achieve
compliance by implementing only technical infrastructure; they must also
establish the human processes, training, and organizational capabilities
necessary to support software update engineering.

APPLICABILITY DIMENSION 5 - SOFTWARE:

The standard applies to "software that can be updated." The term "software"
is defined as "computer programs and associated data intended for installation
on vehicles, vehicle systems, or ECUs that may be dynamically written or
modified during execution." This definition encompasses:

- Application software executing on vehicle systems
- Operating systems and hypervisors running on domain controllers
- Firmware embedded in ECUs and sensors
- Calibration data and configuration parameters
- Machine learning models and neural network weights
- Security credentials and cryptographic material
- User interface components and localization data
- Communication protocol stacks
- Diagnostic software and self-test routines

The phrase "software that can be updated" implies that the standard applies
to software for which update mechanisms exist, regardless of whether updates
have actually occurred or are planned. This forward-looking scope ensures
that organizations consider software update engineering requirements even
when developing systems that may not initially support updates.

EXCLUSIONS AND LIMITATIONS:

The scope statement explicitly notes that the standard "does not prescribe
specific technologies or solutions." This principle of technological
neutrality runs throughout the standard and has several important implications:

- Organizations may choose various implementation approaches to meet the
  standard's requirements, provided they can demonstrate that their chosen
  approach achieves the required objectives.

- The standard does not mandate specific update protocols, file formats,
  or communication mechanisms, allowing organizations to adopt approaches
  appropriate to their technical contexts.

- Requirements are expressed in terms of outcomes (what must be achieved)
  rather than implementations (how it must be done), providing flexibility
  while ensuring minimum standards of safety and security.

- The standard does not prescribe specific organizational structures or
  role definitions, recognizing that organizations vary in size, structure,
  and approach to software update engineering.

PRACTICAL IMPLICATIONS OF SCOPE:

Understanding the scope is essential for organizations determining how
ISO 24089:2023 applies to their specific context. Consider the following
scenarios:

SCENARIO 1 - OEM DEVELOPING OTA CAPABILITY:

An OEM developing over-the-air update capability for its vehicle lineup
must apply the standard across all dimensions. The organization must:
- Establish organizational processes compliant with Clause 4
- Implement project management practices per Clause 5
- Develop infrastructure meeting Clause 6 requirements
- Ensure vehicle capabilities satisfy Clause 7 requirements
- Follow package development processes in Clause 8
- Execute campaigns according to Clause 9

SCENARIO 2 - TIER-1 SUPPLIER DEVELOPING UPDATEABLE ECU:

A Tier-1 supplier developing an engine control module with flash update
capability must apply relevant portions of the standard:
- Organizational processes for software update engineering (Clause 4)
- Project planning for the ECU development including update capability (Clause 5)
- Vehicle system level functions within the ECU for processing updates (Clause 7)
- Package assembly processes when providing software updates (Clause 8)

The supplier may not have direct responsibility for infrastructure
(Clause 6) or campaign execution (Clause 9) if these are OEM responsibilities,
but must ensure the ECU supports the OEM's infrastructure and campaign
requirements.

SCENARIO 3 - INFRASTRUCTURE SERVICE PROVIDER:

A company providing cloud-based OTA management services to multiple OEMs
must apply the standard to its infrastructure:
- Organizational processes for the service organization (Clause 4)
- Project management for customer implementations (Clause 5)
- Infrastructure functions meeting Clause 6 requirements

The infrastructure provider must coordinate with OEM customers to ensure
interoperability between infrastructure and vehicle systems, as required
by Clause 5.3.3.

SECTION 1.3: NORMATIVE REFERENCES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Reference: page_005.png

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"The following documents are referred to in the text in such a way that some
or all of their content constitutes requirements of this document. For dated
references, only the edition cited applies. For undated references, the latest
edition of the referenced document (including any amendments) applies.

- ISO 26262-6:2018, Road vehicles — Functional safety — Part 6: Product
  development at the software level
- ISO 26262-8:2018, Road vehicles — Functional safety — Part 8: Supporting
  processes
- ISO/SAE 21434:2021, Road vehicles — Cybersecurity engineering"

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

Normative references are documents that are indispensable for the application
of the referencing standard. When ISO 24089:2023 references these documents,
their requirements become integrated into the ISO 24089 requirements. This
creates an interdependency that organizations must understand and manage.

ISO 26262-6:2018 - PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT AT THE SOFTWARE LEVEL:

ISO 26262-6 addresses the specific requirements for developing safety-critical
software in road vehicles. The standard specifies the processes, methods, and
documentation required to achieve functional safety in software development.
Key aspects relevant to software update engineering include:

- Software safety requirements specification and decomposition
- Software architectural design requirements
- Safety-related software verification and validation
- Software integration and testing requirements
- Configuration management for safety-critical software
- Tool qualification and confidence levels

The connection between ISO 26262-6 and ISO 24089 arises because software
updates can modify safety-critical software. When an update changes software
that implements safety functions, the update engineering process must ensure
that functional safety is maintained. This requires applying appropriate
portions of ISO 26262-6 to the updated software.

Consider a software update that modifies the braking system control algorithm.
The original development of this software followed ISO 26262-6 requirements
to achieve ASIL (Automotive Safety Integrity Level) classification. The
software update process must ensure that the modified software still meets
its ASIL requirements. This means the update process must include:

- Verification that the updated software satisfies its safety requirements
- Testing appropriate to the ASIL level of the modified software
- Configuration management maintaining traceability between requirements
  and implementation
- Documentation demonstrating that safety has been maintained

ISO 26262-8:2018 - SUPPORTING PROCESSES:

ISO 26262-8 specifies supporting processes that apply across all phases of
the functional safety lifecycle. These processes are essential for software
update engineering because updates may affect safety-critical systems and
must be managed within a robust supporting process framework.

Key supporting processes from ISO 26262-8 relevant to software update
engineering include:

- Interfaces within distributed development: When multiple organizations
  collaborate on software update engineering (such as OEM, Tier-1, and
  infrastructure provider), interfaces must be clearly defined and managed.

- Specification and management of safety requirements: Updated software
  must satisfy its safety requirements; requirements management processes
  ensure traceability and completeness.

- Configuration management: Safety-relevant configuration items must be
  managed throughout the software update lifecycle to maintain integrity
  and traceability.

- Change management: Changes to safety-critical software through updates
  must follow controlled change management processes to assess impact and
  ensure continued safety.

- Verification: Verification activities ensure that updated software
  meets its requirements and that the update process maintains safety.

- Documentation: Safety documentation must be maintained and updated to
  reflect the current state of the vehicle after software updates.

- Confidence in the use of software tools: Tools used in software update
  engineering for safety-critical systems must be qualified or have
  appropriate confidence established.

- Qualification of software components: When updates introduce new software
  components or modify existing ones, qualification requirements apply.

- Proven in use argument: In some cases, proven-in-use arguments may support
  the use of software components in updated systems.

ISO/SAE 21434:2021 - CYBERSECURITY ENGINEERING:

ISO/SAE 21434 is the definitive standard for automotive cybersecurity
engineering. It provides a comprehensive framework for managing cybersecurity
throughout the vehicle lifecycle. The standard's integration into ISO 24089
reflects the fundamental importance of cybersecurity in software update
engineering.

Software updates are inherently cybersecurity-sensitive activities because
they involve:

- Transmitting software over potentially insecure channels
- Authenticating the source and integrity of software packages
- Authorizing update operations to prevent unauthorized modifications
- Protecting update mechanisms from exploitation
- Maintaining the security posture of the vehicle after updates

Key aspects of ISO/SAE 21434 relevant to software update engineering:

- Governance and culture: Organizations must establish cybersecurity
  governance appropriate to their role in software update engineering.

- Risk management: Cybersecurity risks associated with software updates
  must be identified, assessed, and treated throughout the update lifecycle.

- Threat analysis: Potential threats to the software update process must
  be analyzed and appropriate countermeasures implemented.

- Security by design: Update systems must be designed with security
  as a fundamental requirement, not added as an afterthought.

- Validation and verification: Security requirements for update systems
  must be validated and verified to ensure effectiveness.

- Post-production management: Cybersecurity monitoring and incident
  response processes must encompass the software update capability.

The requirement in ISO 24089 Clause 4.3.1.3 to implement software update
engineering activities in accordance with ISO/SAE 21434 means that all
cybersecurity engineering activities described in ISO/SAE 21434 must be
applied to software update engineering, scaled appropriately to the
organization's role and the system's criticality.

IMPLICATIONS OF NORMATIVE REFERENCES:

The normative references create several important obligations for
organizations implementing ISO 24089:

1. COMPETENCY REQUIREMENTS: Organizations must develop competencies in
   functional safety (ISO 26262) and cybersecurity (ISO/SAE 21434) engineering
   to effectively implement software update engineering. This may require
   training, hiring, or engaging expert resources.

2. PROCESS INTEGRATION: Processes for software update engineering must
   integrate with existing functional safety and cybersecurity processes.
   Organizations cannot maintain separate, parallel processes; instead,
   they must create cohesive processes that address all relevant requirements.

3. DOCUMENTATION BURDEN: Documentation requirements from the normatively
   referenced standards apply to software update engineering activities.
   Organizations must establish documentation systems that capture required
   information for all three standards.

4. AUDIT AND ASSESSMENT: Audits of software update engineering processes
   may assess compliance with ISO 24089, ISO 26262, and ISO/SAE 21434
   requirements, depending on the scope and purpose of the audit.

5. SUPPLY CHAIN COMMUNICATION: Requirements must flow down to suppliers
   and partners who participate in software update engineering. Organizations
   must ensure their supply chain understands and can meet requirements
   from all applicable standards.

SECTION 1.4.1: GENERAL TERMINOLOGY (3.1)
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

TERM 3.1.1 - COMPATIBILITY

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Capability of software to be executable on vehicle systems without conflicts.
Note: Can be checked via vehicle configuration information."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Compatibility is a foundational concept in software update engineering that
addresses whether a given software version can properly execute on a specific
vehicle system configuration. The concept encompasses multiple dimensions of
compatibility that must be considered when planning and executing software
updates.

TECHNICAL DIMENSIONS OF COMPATIBILITY:

1. Hardware Compatibility: The software must be designed for the specific
   hardware platform on which it will execute. This includes:
   - Processor architecture and instruction set compatibility
   - Memory map alignment with physical memory configuration
   - Peripheral interface compatibility (SPI, I2C, CAN, LIN, etc.)
   - Pin configuration and signal routing
   - Power supply requirements and voltage levels
   - Clock frequencies and timing parameters

2. Software Architecture Compatibility: The software must integrate with
   the existing software architecture:
   - Operating system or runtime environment compatibility
   - Application programming interface (API) compatibility
   - Communication protocol compatibility (network management,
     diagnostic protocols, application protocols)
   - Data format compatibility (byte ordering, alignment, encoding)
   - Resource usage compatibility (memory allocation, CPU utilization)

3. Functional Compatibility: The updated software must provide expected
   functionality in the vehicle context:
   - Feature availability matching vehicle configuration
   - Calibration compatibility with vehicle-specific parameters
   - Integration with other vehicle systems
   - User interface consistency with vehicle HMI design
   - Regulatory compliance for specific markets

4. Version Compatibility: The software must be compatible with the current
   state of the vehicle:
   - Baseline software version compatibility (whether the update can be
     applied to the current software version)
   - Dependency compatibility (whether required supporting software is
     present at appropriate versions)
   - Configuration data compatibility (whether existing configuration
     data is compatible with the new software)

COMPATIBILITY VERIFICATION METHODS:

The standard notes that compatibility "can be checked via vehicle configuration
information." This implies several verification approaches:

1. Pre-Update Compatibility Checking: Before initiating a software update,
   the vehicle or infrastructure can check the current vehicle configuration
   against compatibility requirements specified in the update package metadata.

   Example: An update package for an engine control module might specify
   that it is compatible with vehicles having:
   - Engine type: 2.0L turbocharged gasoline
   - Transmission: 8-speed automatic
   - Market: North America
   - Current software version: Between v3.2.1 and v3.5.0
   
   The vehicle configuration information must be checked to verify all
   these conditions before the update is allowed to proceed.

2. Hardware Variant Identification: Vehicle configuration information
   identifies hardware variants through part numbers, variant codes, or
   feature flags. Update packages specify compatible hardware variants.

   Example: An infotainment system update might specify compatibility
   with display panel part numbers P/N 12345-A, P/N 12345-B, and P/N
   12345-C, but not P/N 12345-D which has different resolution and
   requires different graphics assets.

3. Feature Set Verification: Vehicle configuration information indicates
   which optional features are present. Updates for feature-specific
   software verify the presence of required features.

   Example: An advanced driver assistance system (ADAS) update might
   require the presence of specific sensor packages (front radar,
   front camera, ultrasonic sensors) that are indicated in the vehicle
   configuration information.

COMPATIBILITY IN PRACTICE - REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES:

EXAMPLE 1: POWERTRAIN CONTROL MODULE UPDATE

An OEM develops a software update for the powertrain control module (PCM)
that improves fuel efficiency through optimized shift scheduling. The
compatibility requirements include:

- Vehicle model year: 2023-2024
- Engine variant: 2.5L naturally aspirated 4-cylinder
- Transmission: CVT with specific controller hardware revision
- Current PCM software version: v4.1.0 or higher
- Absence of certain aftermarket modifications

The update package metadata includes these compatibility constraints.
When the update is distributed, the vehicle configuration information is
checked to verify all conditions. A vehicle with a different engine variant
would be excluded from the update campaign. A vehicle with an older PCM
software version might need an intermediate update first.

EXAMPLE 2: INFOTAINMENT SYSTEM UPDATE

An infotainment system update adds support for a new smartphone integration
protocol. Compatibility requirements include:

- Head unit hardware revision: Rev 2.0 or higher
- Operating system version: Automotive OS v5.x
- Available storage: Minimum 500MB free space
- Display resolution: 1920x1080

The vehicle configuration information provides the head unit hardware
revision and storage capacity. The update process checks these before
allowing installation. Vehicles with older head unit hardware cannot
install the update but continue to function with their existing software.

EXAMPLE 3: BRAKING SYSTEM ECU UPDATE

A braking system ECU update addresses a safety concern identified in
field monitoring. Compatibility requirements include:

- ECU hardware part number: Specific list of compatible part numbers
- Current software version: v2.0.x (specific minor versions)
- Brake system type: Regenerative braking with specific configuration
- Market: Vehicles in specific regulatory jurisdictions

This update requires careful compatibility verification because applying
incorrect software to a braking system could have severe safety consequences.
The vehicle configuration information must be checked against all
compatibility requirements before any installation activity begins.

COMPATIBILITY MANAGEMENT CHALLENGES:

Organizations face several challenges in managing compatibility for
software updates:

1. VARIANT PROLIFERATION: Modern vehicles may have thousands of possible
   configuration variants. Ensuring that update packages correctly specify
   all compatibility constraints and that vehicle configuration information
   accurately reflects the vehicle state requires robust processes.

2. PART NUMBER CHANGES: Hardware components may change during production
   due to supplier changes, cost reductions, or running engineering changes.
   Vehicle configuration information must accurately reflect these changes
   for compatibility checking to be reliable.

3. DEPENDENCY CHAINS: Software components may have complex dependencies
   where updating one component requires updates to related components.
   Compatibility management must address these dependency chains.

4. MARKET-SPECIFIC REQUIREMENTS: Vehicles configured for different markets
   may have different regulatory requirements, feature sets, and hardware.
   Compatibility management must account for market-specific factors.

5. TEMPORAL COMPATIBILITY: An update may be compatible today but become
   incompatible if other updates are applied in the future. Compatibility
   management must consider the temporal dimension.

TERM 3.1.2 - CONDITION

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Criteria required for a software update operation to be completed successfully.
EXAMPLES: Skilled person present, safe vehicle state, in-vehicle resources
available, external resources available."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Conditions represent the prerequisites that must be satisfied for a software
update operation to execute safely and successfully. The standard explicitly
identifies that conditions are required for completion, meaning that the
update operation cannot successfully finish until all specified conditions
are met.

This concept is central to the safe execution of software updates, as it
ensures that updates only proceed when appropriate circumstances exist.
Conditions serve as safeguards against attempting updates in unsafe or
inappropriate situations.

CATEGORIES OF CONDITIONS:

1. HUMAN PRESENCE CONDITIONS:

   Some software updates require human involvement during the update process.
   
   a) Skilled Person Present: Certain updates require a trained technician
      or authorized person to be physically present during the update.
      This condition may be required when:
      - The update process involves steps that require human judgment
      - Manual steps must be performed during the update (such as
        pressing brake pedal, turning ignition key)
      - Immediate intervention may be required if issues arise
      - The update changes safety-critical systems requiring oversight
      
      Example: An update to the electronic power steering system may require
      a skilled person to verify proper steering operation after the update
      and before the vehicle is returned to service.
   
   b) Vehicle User Present: Some updates require the vehicle owner or
      operator to be present to provide consent or perform actions.
      
      Example: An update that changes infotainment system functionality
      may require the user to acknowledge the changes and set preferences
      before the update completes.

2. VEHICLE STATE CONDITIONS:

   The vehicle must be in an appropriate state for the update to proceed.
   
   a) Safe Vehicle State: The standard defines this as "vehicle operating
      mode based on conditions for performing software update operations
      without unreasonable level of risk." This typically means:
      - Vehicle is stationary (not being driven)
      - Transmission is in Park
      - Parking brake is engaged
      - Ignition is in an appropriate state (varies by vehicle architecture)
      - No active safety-critical operations are in progress
      - Environment is appropriate (not during extreme weather, etc.)
      
      Example: An engine control module update may require the engine to
      be off, vehicle stationary, and ambient temperature within a specified
      range to ensure safe programming conditions.
   
   b) Power State Conditions: Updates may require specific power conditions:
      - Sufficient battery charge for the update duration
      - External power connected for extended updates
      - Specific ignition states or power modes
      
      Example: An update that takes 45 minutes and requires the vehicle to
      remain in a specific power state may condition execution on connection
      to a battery maintainer or external power source.

3. IN-VEHICLE RESOURCE CONDITIONS:

   Updates require certain vehicle resources to be available.
   
   a) Computational Resources: Processing capacity for update operations:
      - Available CPU cycles for update processing
      - Memory (RAM) available for update execution
      - Storage space for downloaded packages
   
   b) Communication Resources: Network capacity for update operations:
      - CAN bus availability and bandwidth
      - Ethernet link availability for high-speed updates
      - Diagnostic interface access
      
   c) Energy Resources: Power availability throughout the update:
      - Battery state of charge
      - Charging status (for electric vehicles)
      - Power supply stability
      
      Example: An update to the battery management system of an electric
      vehicle may require the vehicle to be plugged in and charging to
      ensure stable power throughout the update process.

4. EXTERNAL RESOURCE CONDITIONS:

   Some updates require resources external to the vehicle.
   
   a) Communication Resources:
      - Cellular network connectivity for OTA downloads
      - Wi-Fi connectivity for large updates
      - Stable network connection throughout the update process
      
      Example: A 2GB infotainment system update may require Wi-Fi
      connectivity to download the package and may condition execution
      on network stability.
   
   b) Infrastructure Resources:
      - Backend system availability
      - Update server accessibility
      - Authentication infrastructure availability
      
   c) Service Resources:
      - Diagnostic tool availability (for workshop updates)
      - USB flash drive availability (for manual updates)
      - Special equipment availability

CONDITION SPECIFICATION AND VERIFICATION:

Conditions are typically specified in the metadata of the software update
package. The standard requires identification of conditions in Clause
8.3.1.5: "Necessary in-vehicle resources and conditions in target shall
be identified."

The verification of conditions can occur at multiple points:

1. PRE-DOWNLOAD: Before downloading an update package, conditions related
   to connectivity and infrastructure availability are verified.

2. PRE-INSTALLATION: Before initiating the installation phase, conditions
   related to vehicle state and in-vehicle resources are verified.

3. PRE-ACTIVATION: Before activating the new software, conditions related
   to safe activation are verified.

CONDITION ENFORCEMENT APPROACHES:

Organizations implement condition enforcement through various technical
mechanisms:

1. VEHICLE-SIDE ENFORCEMENT: The vehicle's update system checks conditions
   and prevents the update from proceeding if conditions are not met.
   This provides robust protection against unsafe update attempts.

2. INFRASTRUCTURE-SIDE ENFORCEMENT: The backend systems check reported
   vehicle state before authorizing or distributing updates. This can
   prevent unnecessary distribution of updates to vehicles where conditions
   are not met.

3. HYBRID ENFORCEMENT: A combination of vehicle and infrastructure
   enforcement provides defense in depth.

CONDITION HANDLING IN CAMPAIGN PLANNING:

When planning software update campaigns, organizations must consider
conditions to determine:

- Which distribution methods are appropriate (OTA vs. workshop)
- Whether skilled person involvement is required
- What vehicle user communications are necessary
- How to handle updates that cannot proceed due to unmet conditions

CONDITIONS IN PRACTICE - REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES:

EXAMPLE 1: AUTOMATIC EMERGENCY BRAKING UPDATE

A software update improves the performance of the automatic emergency braking
(AEB) system. The conditions specified in the update package metadata include:

- Vehicle speed: 0 km/h (vehicle must be stationary)
- Transmission: Park position
- Parking brake: Engaged
- Engine: Running (to maintain electrical power)
- Front sensors: Clean and unobstructed
- Skilled person: Present (technician must verify sensor alignment after)

This update would be distributed through the workshop network rather than
OTA because of the skilled person requirement.

EXAMPLE 2: INFOTAINMENT SYSTEM FEATURE UPDATE

A software update adds new features to the infotainment system. Conditions
include:

- Vehicle: Parked, ignition in Accessory mode
- Battery: Minimum 60% state of charge, or connected to external power
- Storage: Minimum 2GB free space
- Network: Wi-Fi connected (package size 3GB)
- Vehicle user: Confirmation obtained

This update can be performed OTA when the vehicle is parked at home with
Wi-Fi connectivity and sufficient battery charge.

EXAMPLE 3: BATTERY MANAGEMENT SYSTEM UPDATE

A software update for an electric vehicle's battery management system
optimizes charging algorithms. Conditions include:

- Vehicle: Not charging, charge port door closed
- Battery: State of charge between 20% and 80%
- Temperature: Battery temperature between 10°C and 35°C
- High voltage: System disabled (contactors open)
- External power: Not connected
- Skilled person: Not required

The update can be performed OTA when the vehicle is parked in an appropriate
environmental condition with the battery in the specified state of charge.

CONDITION VIOLATION CONSEQUENCES:

When conditions are not met, several outcomes are possible:

1. UPDATE PREVENTED: The update is not allowed to start or continue until
   conditions are met. This is the safest approach but may delay updates.

2. UPDATE SUSPENDED: The update is paused when a condition becomes unmet
   and can resume when conditions are restored. This requires robust
   suspension and resumption capabilities.

3. UPDATE ROLLED BACK: If an update has partially completed when a condition
   becomes unmet, the system may roll back to the previous state. This
   requires rollback capability in the update mechanism.

4. USER NOTIFICATION: The vehicle user is informed that conditions are
   not met and is directed to take appropriate action.

The standard addresses update failure handling in Clause 7.3.4.10 and
9.3.2.14, requiring functions to ensure vehicle safety if software update
operations fail and specifying corrective actions for update failures.

TERM 3.1.3 - CORRECTIVE ACTION

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Action to eliminate or contain a problem or failure."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Corrective actions in the context of software update engineering address
problems or failures that occur during software update operations. The
standard uses this term specifically in Clause 9.3.1.10, which requires
organizations to "specify corrective actions to be performed in event of
software update operation failure in vehicle."

The concept of corrective action draws from quality management principles
established in standards such as ISO 9001 and IATF 16949, but has specific
application in the software update context.

TYPES OF PROBLEMS AND FAILURES:

1. DOWNLOAD FAILURES:
   - Network connectivity interruption during package download
   - Server unavailability preventing package access
   - Storage failure preventing package storage on vehicle
   - Authentication failure preventing package access
   - Package integrity check failure after download

2. INSTALLATION FAILURES:
   - Insufficient storage space for installation
   - Write failure during flash programming
   - Power interruption during installation
   - Communication failure during multi-ECU installation
   - Timeout during installation steps
   - Hardware incompatibility discovered during installation
   - Unexpected configuration state causing installation failure

3. ACTIVATION FAILURES:
   - Verification failure of installed software
   - Authentication failure of software signature
   - Compatibility check failure at activation time
   - Dependency failure (required software not present)
   - Configuration validation failure

4. POST-UPDATE FAILURES:
   - Software malfunction after activation
   - Integration failure with other vehicle systems
   - Performance degradation after update
   - Safety function impairment after update

CORRECTIVE ACTION CATEGORIES:

1. TECHNICAL CORRECTIVE ACTIONS:

   a) ROLLBACK: Return the affected system to its previous software state.
      This requires:
      - Backup of previous software before update
      - Rollback mechanism that can restore previous state
      - Verification that rollback was successful
      - Notification to infrastructure of rollback event
      
   b) RETRY: Attempt the update operation again after addressing the
      cause of failure.
      - Simple retry (same conditions, same package)
      - Retry with modified conditions
      - Retry with alternative distribution method
      
   c) ALTERNATIVE UPDATE PATH: Use a different approach to accomplish
      the update.
      - Switch from OTA to workshop update
      - Use diagnostic tool instead of self-update
      - Apply intermediate update before target update
      
   d) SYSTEM RECOVERY: Restore system to a functional state when
      normal rollback is not possible.
      - Recovery mode update
      - Hardware replacement (in extreme cases)
      - Factory reset or reinitialization

2. HUMAN CORRECTIVE ACTIONS:

   a) SKILLED PERSON INTERVENTION: Technician performs corrective actions.
      - Manual recovery procedures
      - Diagnostic troubleshooting
      - Forced update or recovery operation
      
   b) VEHICLE USER ACTIONS: User takes specified actions.
      - Perform specific vehicle operations (restart, drive cycle)
      - Bring vehicle to service location
      - Contact customer support
      
   c) ORGANIZATION ACTIONS: Update engineering organization takes action.
      - Modify update package
      - Change campaign parameters
      - Suspend or abort campaign

3. INFRASTRUCTURE CORRECTIVE ACTIONS:

   a) PACKAGE MODIFICATION: Update the software update package.
      - Fix bugs in update logic
      - Adjust compatibility constraints
      - Modify installation parameters
      
   b) CAMPAIGN ADJUSTMENT: Modify campaign parameters.
      - Change target vehicle population
      - Adjust rollout timing or pacing
      - Modify distribution method selection
      
   c) INFRASTRUCTURE MODIFICATION: Adjust backend systems.
      - Increase server capacity
      - Modify network routing
      - Adjust security parameters

CORRECTIVE ACTION PLANNING:

The standard requires corrective actions to be specified in the campaign
plan (Clause 9.3.1.10 and 9.3.1.15). This proactive planning ensures that
organizations are prepared to respond to failures when they occur.

Corrective action planning should consider:

1. FAILURE MODE ANALYSIS: What types of failures might occur?

2. DETECTION METHODS: How will failures be detected?

3. RESPONSE PROCEDURES: What actions should be taken for each failure type?

4. RESPONSIBILITIES: Who is responsible for each corrective action?

5. ESCALATION PATHS: When and how should issues be escalated?

6. COMMUNICATION: Who needs to be informed of failures and corrective actions?

CORRECTIVE ACTION EXAMPLES IN PRACTICE:

EXAMPLE 1: OTA INSTALLATION FAILURE - ROLLBACK

A vehicle fails during installation of an engine control module update due
to a power interruption. The corrective actions include:

- Automatic: Vehicle detects installation failure and initiates rollback
  to previous software version
- Automatic: Rollback completes, system verifies previous version is functional
- Automatic: Notification sent to backend system about failure and rollback
- Infrastructure: Campaign management system flags vehicle for follow-up
- Manual: Vehicle user notified of update failure, advised to retry when
  conditions are met

EXAMPLE 2: COMPATIBILITY CHECK FAILURE - ALTERNATIVE PATH

A vehicle attempts to install an update but fails the compatibility check
due to an unexpected hardware variant. Corrective actions include:

- Automatic: Installation prevented, previous software remains active
- Automatic: Error report sent to backend with vehicle configuration details
- Infrastructure: Engineering team analyzes configuration to understand issue
- Infrastructure: Update package modified to include additional hardware variants
- Automatic: New package distributed to affected vehicle
- Manual: Vehicle user notified that update is available

EXAMPLE 3: POST-UPDATE MALFUNCTION - WORKSHOP REPAIR

A vehicle successfully installs a transmission control update but exhibits
erratic shifting behavior afterward. Corrective actions include:

- Vehicle user: Reports issue to dealer
- Dealer: Technician diagnoses issue, determines software-related problem
- Technician: Initiates forced rollback to previous software version
- Technician: Verifies proper vehicle operation after rollback
- Dealer: Reports issue to OEM through field feedback process
- OEM: Investigates issue, develops corrected update
- OEM: Distributes corrected update through campaign

TERM 3.1.4 - CYBERSECURITY

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Road vehicle cybersecurity - context where assets are sufficiently protected
against threat scenarios to vehicle systems and infrastructure required to
support software update engineering.
Note: Term used instead of 'road vehicle cybersecurity' for brevity."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Cybersecurity in the context of ISO 24089 specifically relates to protecting
assets relevant to software update engineering. This focused definition
acknowledges that software update engineering presents unique cybersecurity
challenges and requires specific protection measures.

The definition draws from ISO/SAE 21434, which provides comprehensive guidance
on automotive cybersecurity engineering. ISO 24089 applies these principles
specifically to the software update domain.

ASSETS REQUIRING PROTECTION:

1. SOFTWARE UPDATE PACKAGES:
   - Integrity: Packages must not be modified in transit or storage
   - Authenticity: Source of packages must be verifiable
   - Confidentiality: Some packages may contain proprietary algorithms
     or sensitive information
   - Availability: Packages must be accessible when needed for updates

2. VEHICLE CONFIGURATION INFORMATION:
   - Confidentiality: Configuration may reveal vehicle capabilities
   - Integrity: Incorrect configuration could enable inappropriate updates
   - Availability: Configuration information must be accessible for
     compatibility checking

3. UPDATE INFRASTRUCTURE:
   - Availability: Infrastructure must be operational to support campaigns
   - Integrity: Infrastructure must not be compromised to distribute
     malicious updates
   - Confidentiality: Infrastructure may contain sensitive vehicle data

4. UPDATE MECHANISMS IN VEHICLES:
   - Integrity: Update mechanisms must not be bypassed or manipulated
   - Availability: Update mechanisms must be functional when needed
   - Authorization: Only authorized updates must be executable

5. CRYPTOGRAPHIC MATERIAL:
   - Private keys for signing update packages
   - Root certificates for package verification
   - Communication keys for secure sessions
   - Authentication credentials

THREAT SCENARIOS FOR SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING:

1. MALICIOUS UPDATE DISTRIBUTION:
   - Attacker compromises infrastructure to distribute malicious software
   - Attacker creates counterfeit update packages
   - Attacker modifies legitimate packages in transit
   
2. UNAUTHORIZED ACCESS:
   - Attacker gains access to infrastructure systems
   - Attacker extracts vehicle or customer data from backend
   - Attacker obtains cryptographic keys
   
3. DENIAL OF SERVICE:
   - Attacker prevents update distribution through infrastructure attack
   - Attacker prevents vehicle from receiving critical safety updates
   - Attacker consumes resources to prevent legitimate update operations
   
4. MANIPULATION OF UPDATE PROCESS:
   - Attacker modifies vehicle configuration to enable unauthorized updates
   - Attacker bypasses compatibility checks to install inappropriate software
   - Attacker triggers rollback to vulnerable software version
   
5. DATA EXTRACTION:
   - Attacker extracts proprietary software through update process
   - Attacker obtains vehicle configuration data revealing capabilities
   - Attacker accesses customer data through update systems

CYBERSECURITY CONTROLS FOR SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING:

1. CRYPTOGRAPHIC CONTROLS:
   - Digital signatures for package authentication
   - Encryption for package confidentiality
   - Secure key management and storage
   - Certificate management and revocation
   
2. ACCESS CONTROL:
   - Authentication for infrastructure access
   - Authorization controls for campaign management
   - Role-based access for update engineering functions
   - Audit logging of access and actions
   
3. SECURE COMMUNICATION:
   - Encrypted channels for package distribution
   - Mutual authentication for vehicle-backend communication
   - Certificate pinning to prevent man-in-middle attacks
   - Secure session management
   
4. VEHICLE-SIDE CONTROLS:
   - Secure boot to verify software integrity
   - Hardware security modules for key storage
   - Secure update orchestration in vehicle
   - Protected update agent execution environment
   
5. INFRASTRUCTURE CONTROLS:
   - Network segmentation and firewalling
   - Intrusion detection and prevention
   - Security monitoring and alerting
   - Incident response capabilities

RELATIONSHIP TO ISO/SAE 21434:

ISO 24089 normatively references ISO/SAE 21434, meaning organizations must
apply ISO/SAE 21434 principles to software update engineering. Key
requirements from ISO/SAE 21434 that apply include:

- Cybersecurity governance and organizational processes
- Risk assessment and treatment for update systems
- Security requirements definition for update functions
- Secure design of update architectures
- Verification and validation of security measures
- Monitoring and incident response for update systems
- Post-production cybersecurity management

CYBERSECURITY IN PRACTICE - REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES:

EXAMPLE 1: OTA UPDATE SIGNING AND VERIFICATION

An OEM implements cryptographic signing for all software update packages:

- Development: Software builds are signed with development keys
- Release: Production signing with hardware security module (HSM) protected keys
- Distribution: Packages distributed with signature attached
- Vehicle verification: Each ECU verifies signature before installation
- Certificate chain: Vehicle contains root certificates, verifies chain
  to trusted root

This implementation addresses the threat of malicious update distribution
by ensuring that only packages signed by the OEM can be installed.

EXAMPLE 2: SECURE COMMUNICATION FOR OTA

An OEM implements secure communication for OTA updates:

- Mutual TLS: Vehicle and backend mutually authenticate using certificates
- Certificate management: Vehicles provisioned with certificates at manufacture
- Session security: Unique session keys established for each session
- Certificate renewal: Vehicles can renew certificates before expiration
- Certificate revocation: Backend maintains revocation lists for compromised
  certificates

This implementation addresses threats of man-in-middle attacks and
unauthorized access to update communications.

EXAMPLE 3: INFRASTRUCTURE SECURITY CONTROLS

An OEM implements security controls for OTA infrastructure:

- Network segmentation: Update systems isolated from other enterprise networks
- Access control: Multi-factor authentication for administrative access
- Monitoring: Security information and event management (SIEM) monitors
  all infrastructure activity
- Incident response: Defined procedures for security incident handling
- Penetration testing: Regular third-party testing of infrastructure

This implementation addresses threats of infrastructure compromise and
unauthorized access to update systems.

TERM 3.1.5 - CYBERSECURITY RISK

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Effect of uncertainty on cybersecurity expressed in terms of attack
feasibility and impact.
Source: ISO/SAE 21434:2021, 3.1.29"

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Cybersecurity risk in the context of software update engineering represents
the potential for harm resulting from successful cyber attacks on update
systems or processes. The definition emphasizes two key dimensions:

1. ATTACK FEASIBILITY: How likely is it that an attack will succeed?
2. IMPACT: What are the consequences if the attack succeeds?

This definition aligns with ISO/SAE 21434's risk management approach and
provides the foundation for risk-based decision making in software update
engineering.

CYBERSECURITY RISK ASSESSMENT FOR SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING:

Organizations must assess cybersecurity risks throughout the software update
lifecycle. Key activities include:

1. THREAT SCENARIO IDENTIFICATION:
   - What attacks are possible against update systems?
   - Who are potential threat actors (hackers, insiders, nation-states)?
   - What are their motivations and capabilities?
   - What attack vectors are available?

2. ATTACK FEASIBILITY ASSESSMENT:
   ISO/SAE 21434 provides a methodology for assessing attack feasibility
   based on:
   
   a) Elapsed Time: How long does the attack take?
      - Days, hours, minutes, seconds
   
   b) Specialist Expertise: What expertise is needed?
      - Layman, proficient, expert, multiple experts
   
   c) Knowledge of the Item: How much system knowledge is required?
      - Public, restricted, sensitive, critical
   
   d) Window of Opportunity: When can the attack be executed?
      - Unlimited, easy, moderate, difficult
   
   e) Equipment: What equipment is needed?
      - Standard, specialized, bespoke, multiple bespoke

3. IMPACT ASSESSMENT:
   Impacts are assessed across multiple dimensions:
   
   a) Safety Impact: Could the attack cause physical harm?
      - Injuries, fatalities, property damage
   
   b) Financial Impact: What are the monetary consequences?
      - Repair costs, liability, brand damage, regulatory fines
   
   c) Operational Impact: How does it affect operations?
      - Update capability disruption, vehicle availability
   
   d) Privacy Impact: Is personal data compromised?
      - Customer information exposure, regulatory violations

4. RISK EVALUATION:
   Combining attack feasibility and impact produces a risk level:
   
   - Very High Risk: High feasibility + High impact
   - High Risk: Moderate feasibility + High impact, or High feasibility + Moderate impact
   - Medium Risk: Various combinations
   - Low Risk: Low feasibility + Low impact

CYBERSECURITY RISK TREATMENT:

Organizations must treat identified risks through appropriate measures:

1. RISK AVOIDANCE: Eliminate the risk by not performing the activity
   Example: Not distributing certain updates OTA due to unacceptable risk

2. RISK REDUCTION: Reduce risk through implementing controls
   Example: Adding cryptographic verification to reduce risk of malicious updates

3. RISK TRANSFER: Transfer risk to another party
   Example: Cyber insurance, third-party security services

4. RISK RETENTION: Accept the residual risk after treatment
   Example: Documenting acceptance of residual risk with justification

RISK MANAGEMENT IN SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING CONTEXT:

Clause 6.3.1.1 requires organizations to "manage cybersecurity risks of
infrastructure." Clause 7.3.1.3 requires that "cybersecurity risks of
software update operations in vehicle shall be managed."

These requirements establish that cybersecurity risk management is mandatory
at both infrastructure and vehicle levels. Organizations must demonstrate
that they have:

- Identified relevant cybersecurity risks
- Assessed risk levels using appropriate methodology
- Implemented risk treatment measures
- Documented risk management decisions
- Monitored for changes in risk landscape

CYBERSECURITY RISK EXAMPLES IN PRACTICE:

EXAMPLE 1: INFRASTRUCTURE COMPROMISE RISK

Threat Scenario: Attacker compromises OTA backend to distribute malicious
software to vehicles.

Attack Feasibility Assessment:
- Elapsed Time: Days to weeks (requires sustained access)
- Specialist Expertise: Expert (requires infrastructure security expertise)
- Knowledge: Sensitive (requires detailed system knowledge)
- Window of Opportunity: Moderate (requires bypassing security controls)
- Equipment: Specialized (hacking tools, custom scripts)
Feasibility Rating: Medium

Impact Assessment:
- Safety: High (malicious software could cause safety issues)
- Financial: High (recall, liability, brand damage)
- Operational: High (update capability compromised)
- Privacy: Medium (potential customer data exposure)
Impact Rating: High

Risk Level: HIGH

Treatment: Strong access controls, multi-factor authentication, security
monitoring, intrusion detection, regular penetration testing, incident
response planning.

EXAMPLE 2: VEHICLE UPDATE MECHANISM EXPLOITATION RISK

Threat Scenario: Attacker exploits vulnerability in vehicle update mechanism
to install unauthorized software locally.

Attack Feasibility Assessment:
- Elapsed Time: Hours to days
- Specialist Expertise: Proficient to Expert
- Knowledge: Restricted (requires vehicle architecture knowledge)
- Window of Opportunity: Moderate (requires physical or local network access)
- Equipment: Specialized (diagnostic tools, custom hardware)
Feasibility Rating: Medium

Impact Assessment:
- Safety: Medium to High (unauthorized software on vehicle)
- Financial: Medium (single vehicle affected)
- Operational: Low (isolated to individual vehicles)
- Privacy: Low (limited personal data at vehicle level)
Impact Rating: Medium

Risk Level: MEDIUM

Treatment: Secure boot, hardware-protected update mechanism, signed packages
with strong verification, tamper detection.

TERM 3.1.6 - DEPENDENCY

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Effect of software for one vehicle system on the same or other vehicle
systems.
Note: Can generate a condition in the metadata of a software update package."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Dependencies represent the interconnected nature of vehicle software systems
and the ways in which software changes to one system can affect other systems.
Understanding and managing dependencies is critical for successful software
update engineering.

TYPES OF DEPENDENCIES:

1. FUNCTIONAL DEPENDENCIES:

   a) Service Dependencies: One system provides services used by another.
      Example: ADAS system depends on vehicle dynamics data from the
      chassis control system. An update to chassis control that changes
      the data format requires a corresponding ADAS update.
   
   b) Shared Resource Dependencies: Systems share vehicle resources.
      Example: Multiple systems use vehicle network bandwidth. An update
      that increases network utilization by one system may impact others.
   
   c) Sequencing Dependencies: Updates must be applied in a specific order.
      Example: A gateway update must be applied before updates to systems
      behind the gateway can be installed.

2. INTERFACE DEPENDENCIES:

   a) Communication Dependencies: Systems communicate via defined interfaces.
      Example: Engine control and transmission control communicate via CAN
      messages. An update changing message content requires coordination.
   
   b) Calibration Dependencies: Systems share calibration parameters.
      Example: Engine calibration depends on transmission characteristics.
      An update to either may require recalibration.
   
   c) Configuration Dependencies: Systems depend on configuration data.
      Example: Multiple systems use vehicle configuration flags. An update
      that changes configuration interpretation affects all systems.

3. VERSION DEPENDENCIES:

   a) Minimum Version Dependencies: One system requires minimum version
      of another system.
      Example: Feature X requires software version Y.Z or higher on the
      related controller.
   
   b) Exact Version Dependencies: Systems must have specific versions.
      Example: Communication protocol implementation requires exact matching
      versions across communicating ECUs.
   
   c) Version Range Dependencies: Systems must be within version ranges.
      Example: System A versions 2.0-2.5 are compatible with System B
      versions 3.0-3.2.

4. HARDWARE DEPENDENCIES:

   a) Shared Hardware Dependencies: Systems share hardware components.
      Example: Multiple systems use a shared domain controller. An update
      to the domain controller affects all systems it hosts.
   
   b) Sensor/Actuator Dependencies: Systems share sensors or actuators.
      Example: ADAS and chassis control share vehicle dynamics sensors.
      Changes to sensor processing affect both systems.

DEPENDENCY MANAGEMENT IN SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING:

1. DEPENDENCY IDENTIFICATION (Clause 8.3.1.4):
   "Dependencies for software update package with target shall be determined."
   
   Organizations must identify all dependencies that affect the software
   update package. This includes:
   - Software dependencies (other software versions required)
   - Hardware dependencies (hardware variants required)
   - Configuration dependencies (configuration states required)
   - Timing dependencies (update sequencing requirements)

2. DEPENDENCY DOCUMENTATION:
   Dependencies must be documented in the software update package metadata.
   This enables:
   - Pre-update compatibility checking
   - Proper update sequencing
   - Failure prevention due to unmet dependencies

3. DEPENDENCY VALIDATION (Clause 8.3.3.3):
   Organizations must validate that dependencies are properly specified
   and that the update will function correctly given its dependencies.

4. DEPENDENCY RESOLUTION:
   When dependencies exist, organizations must plan for their resolution:
   - Ensuring dependent updates are available before target update
   - Sequencing updates to satisfy dependencies
   - Bundling updates to simplify dependency management

DEPENDENCY CONDITION GENERATION:

The standard notes that dependencies "can generate a condition in the metadata
of a software update package." This means that dependencies translate into
conditions that must be satisfied for the update to proceed.

Example: Software update package for System A has a dependency on System B
version 3.0 or higher. This translates into a condition: "System B software
version must be >= 3.0." The update for System A cannot proceed until this
condition is verified as satisfied.

DEPENDENCY EXAMPLES IN PRACTICE:

EXAMPLE 1: ADAS CAMERA AND PROCESSING UNIT UPDATE

An OEM develops an update for an ADAS camera module. The camera streams
video data to a separate processing unit that runs the ADAS algorithms.
Dependencies include:

- Processing unit must have software version 4.2 or higher (to handle
  new video format from camera)
- Vehicle must have ADAS feature package (not all variants have ADAS)
- Network gateway must support required bandwidth

The update package for the camera includes these dependencies in its
metadata. When a vehicle receives the camera update, the system checks
that the processing unit is at version 4.2+. If not, the camera update
is deferred until the processing unit is updated.

EXAMPLE 2: INFOTAINMENT AND CONNECTIVITY MODULE UPDATE

An infotainment system update adds new connected services. Dependencies:

- Connectivity module firmware version 2.1+ (supports new protocols)
- Vehicle configuration indicates connected services subscription
- Backend system supports new services

The campaign planning must sequence the connectivity module update before
the infotainment update for vehicles where the connectivity module needs
updating.

EXAMPLE 3: POWERTRAIN INTEGRATED UPDATE

An OEM develops a powertrain update affecting engine control, transmission
control, and hybrid control modules. Dependencies include:

- Engine control and transmission control must be updated together
  (matching calibration sets)
- Hybrid control must be at version 3.x before engine update
- Vehicle must have specific hardware revision of hybrid inverter

The update package is structured to include all three controllers with
metadata specifying the update sequencing requirements. The installation
process ensures all three are installed together as an atomic operation.

TERM 3.1.7 - ECU (ELECTRONIC CONTROL UNIT)

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Embedded device in a vehicle whose software can be updated."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

The ECU is the fundamental unit of automotive electronics, serving as the
building block for vehicle software systems. ISO 24089 specifically defines
ECU in the context of software update capability, emphasizing that the
standard applies to devices that can receive software updates.

ECU ARCHITECTURE AND UPDATE CAPABILITY:

Modern ECUs typically include:

1. PROCESSOR SUBSYSTEM:
   - Microcontroller or microprocessor
   - Flash memory for program storage (updateable)
   - RAM for runtime data
   - Non-volatile memory for configuration/calibration data
   
2. INTERFACES:
   - Vehicle network interfaces (CAN, LIN, FlexRay, Ethernet)
   - Sensor inputs
   - Actuator outputs
   - Diagnostic interface
   
3. POWER MANAGEMENT:
   - Power supply regulation
   - Low-power modes
   - Wake-up mechanisms

The flash memory architecture determines the update capability:

- SINGLE-BANK FLASH: Entire flash must be erased and programmed, requiring
  the ECU to be non-functional during update. Rollback requires external
  storage of previous software.

- DUAL-BANK FLASH: One bank can be updated while the other executes.
  Enables faster updates and easier rollback by switching between banks.

- EXTERNAL FLASH: Additional flash storage for software images enables
  more flexible update strategies.

ECU TYPES IN MODERN VEHICLES:

1. SENSORS AND ACTUATORS:
   - Smart sensors with embedded firmware
   - Actuator controllers
   - Low to medium complexity
   
2. DOMAIN CONTROLLERS:
   - Consolidate multiple vehicle functions
   - Higher processing capability
   - More complex software architecture
   - May host multiple software components
   
3. ZONE CONTROLLERS:
   - Geographically distributed controllers
   - Interface with local sensors/actuators
   - May include gateway functionality
   
4. CENTRAL COMPUTING:
   - High-performance computing platforms
   - Run sophisticated software stacks
   - May use hypervisors or containers
   - Complex update requirements

ECU UPDATE MECHANISMS:

1. DIAGNOSTIC UPDATE:
   - Update via diagnostic protocol (UDS, KWP2000)
   - Requires diagnostic tool connected to vehicle
   - Typically performed at workshop
   - Well-established, reliable approach

2. OTA UPDATE:
   - Update received via wireless communication
   - May be staged in vehicle storage
   - Self-installed by ECU or update manager
   - Enables remote updates without workshop visit

3. BOOTLOADER UPDATE:
   - Update initiated by ECU bootloader
   - May receive image via vehicle network or external interface
   - Basic mechanism used across update approaches

4. RUNTIME UPDATE:
   - Update while ECU continues to execute
   - Requires sophisticated architecture (dual-bank, external storage)
   - Enables shorter vehicle downtime

ECU CONSIDERATIONS FOR SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING:

1. RESOURCE CONSTRAINTS:
   - Limited flash endurance (number of programming cycles)
   - Limited RAM for update operations
   - Timing constraints for update completion
   - Power requirements for flash programming

2. SAFETY CONSIDERATIONS:
   - Safety-critical ECUs require careful update management
   - ASIL-rated ECUs must maintain safety through update process
   - Verification of safety functions after update

3. SECURITY CONSIDERATIONS:
   - Secure boot to verify software authenticity
   - Protected update mechanisms
   - Secure key storage for signature verification

4. NETWORK CONSIDERATIONS:
   - Update traffic on vehicle networks
   - Gateway routing for multi-ECU updates
   - Network topology affecting update paths

ECU EXAMPLES IN PRACTICE:

EXAMPLE 1: ENGINE CONTROL MODULE UPDATE

An engine control module (ECM) with single-bank flash requires a diagnostic
update for safety-critical calibration changes. The update process:

1. Vehicle brought to workshop
2. Technician connects diagnostic tool
3. Current software version read and verified
4. Engine off, ignition on (to power ECM)
5. Flash programming performed via diagnostic protocol
6. ECM verifies new software integrity
7. ECM performs self-test
8. Technician verifies engine operation

The single-bank architecture requires the ECM to be non-functional during
programming, hence the workshop environment.

EXAMPLE 2: INFOTAINMENT HEAD UNIT UPDATE

An infotainment head unit with dual-bank flash receives an OTA update for
new features. The update process:

1. Vehicle receives notification of available update
2. User confirms update initiation
3. Package downloaded to vehicle storage
4. System verifies package integrity and authenticity
5. Update installed to inactive bank while current software runs
6. Installation verified
7. User prompted to restart infotainment
8. System switches to new bank on restart
9. New software activates

The dual-bank architecture enables the update to proceed while the current
software continues to function, minimizing vehicle downtime.

EXAMPLE 3: DOMAIN CONTROLLER UPDATE

A domain controller hosting multiple software components (ADAS, instrument
cluster, body control) receives a comprehensive update. The domain controller
architecture includes:

- Hypervisor for component isolation
- Multiple virtual machines or containers
- External flash for software staging
- Redundant boot mechanisms

The update process:

1. Components of update package extracted
2. Each component verified
3. Components installed to external flash
4. System prepares to switch to new versions
5. Hypervisor reconfigured for new components
6. System reboot to activate new software
7. Rollback capability maintained by keeping previous versions

This architecture supports complex, multi-component updates with rollback
capability and component-level verification.

TERM 3.1.8 - FUNCTIONAL SAFETY

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Absence of unreasonable risk due to hazards caused by malfunctioning
behaviour of vehicle systems.
Source: ISO 26262-1:2018, 3.67, modified."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Functional safety is a fundamental concept in automotive engineering that
ISO 24089 incorporates through its normative reference to ISO 26262. The
definition establishes that functional safety is about managing risks from
malfunctions - situations where systems fail to behave as intended.

UNDERSTANDING UNREASONABLE RISK:

The concept of "unreasonable risk" is central to functional safety. Risk
is inherent in all activities, including driving. Functional safety does
not aim to eliminate all risk (which is impossible) but to reduce risk
to a reasonable level. The determination of what is "reasonable" involves:

1. SOCIETAL ACCEPTANCE: What level of risk does society generally accept
   for transportation?
2. TECHNOLOGICAL FEASIBILITY: What risk reduction is achievable with
   current technology?
3. ECONOMIC CONSIDERATIONS: What is the cost of risk reduction versus
   the benefit?
4. REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS: What do regulations mandate?

MALFUNCTIONING BEHAVIOUR VS. INTENDED FUNCTION:

ISO 26262 distinguishes between:

1. MALFUNCTIONING BEHAVIOUR: System fails to perform as designed
   Examples: Sensor provides incorrect value, software calculates wrong
   result, actuator doesn't respond
   This is the domain of ISO 26262 functional safety

2. INTENDED FUNCTION LIMITATIONS: System performs as designed but
   cannot handle all situations
   Example: ADAS camera correctly detects vehicles but cannot detect
   pedestrians in certain lighting
   This is the domain of ISO 21448 (SOTIF - Safety of the Intended Function)

SOFTWARE UPDATES AND FUNCTIONAL SAFETY:

Software updates can affect functional safety in multiple ways:

1. DIRECT SAFETY IMPACT: Update modifies safety-critical software
   - Changing braking algorithm
   - Modifying steering control logic
   - Updating airbag deployment parameters
   - Each such update must maintain functional safety

2. INDIRECT SAFETY IMPACT: Update affects systems that interact with
   safety-critical systems
   - Communication protocol changes affecting safety data
   - Configuration changes affecting safety system behavior
   - Calibration changes affecting system performance

3. UPDATE PROCESS SAFETY: The update process itself must be safe
   - Vehicle must remain safe during update
   - Failed updates must not leave system in unsafe state
   - Rollback must restore safe operation

FUNCTIONAL SAFETY MANAGEMENT IN SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING:

Organizations must manage functional safety throughout the software update
lifecycle:

1. HAZARD IDENTIFICATION: What hazards could arise from the update?

2. RISK ASSESSMENT: What is the risk level for each hazard?

3. SAFETY REQUIREMENTS: What requirements mitigate the risks?

4. VERIFICATION: How do we verify safety requirements are met?

5. VALIDATION: How do we validate the updated system is safe?

ASIL (AUTOMOTIVE SAFETY INTEGRITY LEVEL) CONSIDERATIONS:

ISO 26262 assigns ASIL ratings (A, B, C, D) to safety requirements based
on severity, exposure, and controllability of hazards. Software updates
must maintain ASIL compliance:

- ASIL-A: Lowest safety requirements
- ASIL-B: Moderate safety requirements
- ASIL-C: High safety requirements
- ASIL-D: Highest safety requirements

Updates to ASIL-rated software require appropriate development rigor
matching the ASIL level.

FUNCTIONAL SAFETY IN PRACTICE - REAL-WORLD EXAMPLES:

EXAMPLE 1: ELECTRONIC POWER STEERING UPDATE

An OEM develops an update for the electronic power steering (EPS) ECU to
improve steering feel. The EPS is ASIL-D rated. Update considerations:

- Development process must follow ASIL-D requirements from ISO 26262-6
- Testing must include comprehensive safety verification
- Documentation must demonstrate ASIL-D compliance
- Update process must maintain steering safety throughout

The update package development includes:
- Safety requirements specification
- Software unit design per ASIL-D guidelines
- Code review and analysis per ASIL-D requirements
- Integration testing including fault injection
- Vehicle-level safety validation

EXAMPLE 2: BRAKE SYSTEM SOFTWARE UPDATE

A brake system software update addresses an identified issue. Safety
considerations:

- Update cannot degrade brake performance
- Update must not introduce new failure modes
- Verification testing at component and vehicle level
- Validation of safety functions after update
- Documentation of safety evidence

The update is distributed via workshop network (not OTA) due to safety
criticality, ensuring technician oversight and verification.

TERM 3.1.9 - FUNCTIONAL SAFETY RISK

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Combination of the probability of occurrence of harm and the severity
of that harm.
Source: ISO 26262-1:2018, 3.128"

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Functional safety risk quantifies the potential for harm from system
malfunctions. The definition uses probability and severity as the two
key parameters for risk assessment.

RISK COMPONENTS:

1. PROBABILITY OF OCCURRENCE:
   How likely is it that the hazardous event will occur?
   Factors affecting probability:
   - Hardware failure rates
   - Software fault probabilities
   - Exposure to operational conditions
   - Usage patterns

2. SEVERITY OF HARM:
   How severe are the consequences if the hazardous event occurs?
   Severity categories typically include:
   - S0: No injuries
   - S1: Light to moderate injuries
   - S2: Severe to life-threatening injuries (survival probable)
   - S3: Life-threatening injuries (survival uncertain) to fatal

RISK ASSESSMENT IN ISO 26262:

ISO 26262 uses three parameters to determine ASIL:

1. SEVERITY (S): S0, S1, S2, S3
2. EXPOSURE (E): E0, E1, E2, E3, E4 (how often the situation occurs)
3. CONTROLLABILITY (C): C0, C1, C2, C3 (can the driver control the hazard)

The combination of S, E, and C determines the ASIL (A, B, C, or D) or
QM (Quality Management - no special safety requirements).

SOFTWARE UPDATE RISK CONSIDERATIONS:

When planning software updates, organizations must consider:

1. RISK INTRODUCTION: Could the update introduce new risks?
2. RISK MODIFICATION: Could the update change existing risk levels?
3. RISK MITIGATION: Does the update address identified risks?

RISK-BASED UPDATE DECISIONS:

The functional safety risk concept supports risk-based decision making:

1. HIGH-RISK UPDATES: Updates to high-ASIL systems require:
   - More rigorous development processes
   - More extensive testing
   - More thorough documentation
   - Often workshop-based installation

2. LOW-RISK UPDATES: Updates to non-safety systems may have:
   - Standard development processes
   - Standard testing
   - Standard documentation
   - OTA distribution acceptable

TERM 3.1.11 - IN-VEHICLE RESOURCE

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Vehicle or ECU available properties relevant for software update engineering.
EXAMPLES: Computational power, network capacity, RAM, storage, battery capacity."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

In-vehicle resources represent the physical and logical assets available
within the vehicle that are necessary for performing software update
operations. Understanding and managing these resources is essential for
successful software update engineering.

RESOURCE CATEGORIES:

1. COMPUTATIONAL RESOURCES:

   a) PROCESSING CAPACITY:
      - CPU cycles available for update operations
      - Processing capability of update manager
      - Ability to handle concurrent operations
      
   b) MEMORY RESOURCES:
      - RAM available for update operations
      - Flash storage for package staging
      - Temporary storage during installation
      
   c) PROCESSING CONSTRAINTS:
      - Real-time requirements may limit update processing
      - Safety-critical systems may have limited availability
      - Power modes affect processing availability

2. NETWORK RESOURCES:

   a) VEHICLE NETWORKS:
      - CAN bus bandwidth for update traffic
      - Ethernet bandwidth for large packages
      - LIN, FlexRay, or other network capacity
      
   b) EXTERNAL CONNECTIVITY:
      - Cellular bandwidth for downloads
      - Wi-Fi bandwidth for large downloads
      - Stability of connection
      
   c) NETWORK CONSTRAINTS:
      - Network traffic from vehicle operations
      - Gateway routing limitations
      - Network priority assignments

3. STORAGE RESOURCES:

   a) PACKAGE STORAGE:
      - Space for downloaded update packages
      - Temporary storage during installation
      - Backup storage for rollback
      
   b) SOFTWARE STORAGE:
      - Flash capacity for new software
      - Dual-bank storage requirements
      - Configuration data storage
      
   c) STORAGE CONSTRAINTS:
      - Limited flash endurance
      - Fragmentation management
      - Wear leveling requirements

4. ENERGY RESOURCES:

   a) BATTERY CAPACITY:
      - Available charge for update operations
      - Power draw during update
      - Duration capability on battery alone
      
   b) POWER SUPPLY:
      - External power availability
      - Charging system status
      - Power stability requirements
      
   c) ENERGY CONSTRAINTS:
      - Low voltage during cranking
      - Load management during update
      - Standby power requirements

RESOURCE MANAGEMENT IN SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING:

ISO 24089 addresses in-vehicle resources in several requirements:

1. Clause 8.3.1.5: "Necessary in-vehicle resources and conditions in target
   shall be identified."
   - Update packages must specify required resources
   - Compatibility checking must verify resource availability

2. Clause 6.3.4.5: "Infrastructure should have one or more functions to
   determine whether there are sufficient in-vehicle resources to apply
   software update package to recipients."
   - Backend should be able to predict resource requirements
   - Pre-checking can prevent failed update attempts

3. Clause 7.3.4.3: "There shall be one or more functions to determine
   that necessary conditions are met to perform software update operations."
   - Vehicle must verify resource availability
   - Updates should not proceed without sufficient resources

RESOURCE CONSIDERATIONS IN PRACTICE:

EXAMPLE 1: STORAGE RESOURCE MANAGEMENT

An OTA update package for an infotainment system is 2.5GB. The vehicle's
download staging area has 4GB available. Resource management includes:

- Check available storage before download initiation
- Monitor storage during download
- Ensure sufficient space for installation (may need more than package size)
- Clean up staging area after successful installation
- Maintain rollback capability (may require additional storage)

EXAMPLE 2: ENERGY RESOURCE MANAGEMENT

An engine control module update requires 30 minutes and significant
power during flash programming. Resource management includes:

- Verify battery state of charge before starting
- Monitor voltage during update
- Recommend external power connection
- Abort if voltage drops below threshold
- Ensure safe rollback on power failure

EXAMPLE 3: NETWORK RESOURCE MANAGEMENT

A multi-ECU update requires substantial vehicle network traffic for
coordinated installation. Resource management includes:

- Schedule update during vehicle idle (minimal network traffic)
- Coordinate update traffic to avoid bus overload
- Gate updates through network manager
- Handle gateway routing appropriately
- Manage timeouts and retries

TERM 3.1.12 - RECIPIENT

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Individual instance of vehicle, vehicle system, or ECU that receives a
software update package during a campaign."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

The concept of recipient is essential for understanding how software
update campaigns operate. While a target defines a class of entities
(e.g., "all vehicles of Model X with Feature Y"), a recipient is a
specific instance (e.g., VIN 123456789 with ECU serial number ABC123).

TARGET VS. RECIPIENT:

TARGET:
- Abstract definition of update scope
- Based on vehicle configuration information
- Defines classes of vehicles/systems/ECUs
- Used in campaign planning and package development

RECIPIENT:
- Concrete instance receiving update
- Specific VIN, ECU, or system
- Derived by resolving targets
- Used in campaign execution

RECIPIENT RESOLUTION:

The process of converting targets to recipients is called recipient
resolution. This process is addressed in ISO 24089:

Clause 6.3.4.3: "Infrastructure shall have one or more functions to resolve
targets into recipients for software update campaigns."

Clause 9.3.2.1: "Vehicle configuration information necessary to resolve
targets into recipients shall be obtained for software update campaign."

Clause 9.3.2.2: "Targets of software update campaign shall be resolved into
recipients."

RECIPIENT RESOLUTION PROCESS:

1. TARGET DEFINITION: Campaign specifies target criteria
   Example: "All 2024 Model Y vehicles with ADAS Package in California"

2. VEHICLE CONFIGURATION QUERY: Infrastructure queries configuration database
   to identify matching vehicles

3. RECIPIENT LIST GENERATION: Specific VINs are identified
   Example: VINs 1A2B3C..., 4D5E6F..., 7G8H9I... match criteria

4. RECIPIENT VALIDATION: Each recipient is validated for applicability
   - Current software version compatible with update
   - Required conditions can be met
   - Update not already applied

5. RECIPIENT PRIORITIZATION: Recipients may be prioritized for staged rollout
   - Safety-critical recipients first
   - Geographic or feature-based prioritization
   - Random or systematic selection

RECIPIENT TRACKING:

During campaign execution, each recipient's status must be tracked:

1. PENDING: Identified but update not yet initiated
2. SCHEDULED: Update scheduled for specific time
3. DOWNLOADING: Package download in progress
4. DOWNLOADED: Package downloaded, installation pending
5. INSTALLING: Installation in progress
6. INSTALLED: Installation complete, activation pending
7. COMPLETED: Update successfully completed
8. FAILED: Update failed, requires corrective action
9. EXCLUDED: Originally identified but later excluded

RECIPIENT EXAMPLES IN PRACTICE:

EXAMPLE 1: OTA CAMPAIGN RECIPIENT MANAGEMENT

An OEM conducts an OTA campaign for infotainment updates:

Target: All 2023-2024 Model X vehicles with Navigation Package
Recipient Resolution: 47,382 matching VINs identified

Campaign execution:
- Week 1: 10,000 recipients scheduled (staged rollout)
- Monitoring: 9,847 complete successfully, 153 fail
- Analysis: Failures due to connectivity issues, low storage
- Remediation: Failed recipients rescheduled
- Week 2: Remaining 37,382 recipients scheduled
- Final: 47,201 recipients updated successfully

EXAMPLE 2: SAFETY RECALL CAMPAIGN RECIPIENT MANAGEMENT

A safety recall requires updating the braking system ECU:

Target: All vehicles with affected brake ECU hardware revision
Recipient Resolution: 12,456 VINs identified across 3 model years

Campaign execution:
- Workshop-based campaign (not OTA due to safety criticality)
- Each VIN tracked from notification through completion
- Owner notifications sent in batches
- Dealer appointments scheduled
- Technician updates logged per VIN
- Completion tracked for regulatory reporting

TERM 3.1.13 - SAFE VEHICLE STATE

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Vehicle operating mode based on conditions for performing software update
operations without unreasonable level of risk.
Notes: Can vary based on required conditions for the software update package.
Can vary based on the software update operation step being performed."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Safe vehicle state is a critical concept that ensures software update
operations are performed only when the vehicle is in an appropriate
condition. The definition acknowledges that what constitutes a "safe
state" can vary depending on the specific update and the phase of the
update operation.

DETERMINING SAFE VEHICLE STATE:

Safe vehicle state is determined by considering:

1. VEHICLE DYNAMICS:
   - Is the vehicle moving or stationary?
   - What is the vehicle's location (road, workshop, garage)?
   - What is the vehicle's attitude (level ground, incline)?

2. POWER STATE:
   - Is the engine running?
   - What is the ignition state?
   - Is external power connected?
   - What is the battery state of charge?

3. SYSTEM STATUS:
   - Are safety-critical systems operational?
   - What vehicle functions are active?
   - What systems are available for update?

4. ENVIRONMENT:
   - What is the ambient temperature?
   - Are there environmental hazards?
   - What is the communication environment?

SAFE STATE VARIATIONS:

The standard notes that safe vehicle state can vary:

1. BASED ON SOFTWARE UPDATE PACKAGE:
   Different updates require different safe states:
   
   - Infotainment update: Vehicle parked, ignition in accessory, sufficient
     battery charge
   
   - Engine control update: Engine off, vehicle stationary, level ground,
     no active diagnostic sessions
   
   - Brake system update: Vehicle stationary, park brake engaged, technician
     present, diagnostic tool connected

2. BASED ON UPDATE OPERATION STEP:
   Safe state may change during the update process:
   
   - DOWNLOAD phase: Vehicle can be in normal operation, downloading in
     background
   
   - INSTALLATION phase: Certain systems may need to be quiescent
   
   - ACTIVATION phase: Vehicle may need to be in specific power state,
     some functions unavailable

SAFE STATE REQUIREMENTS IN ISO 24089:

Clause 7.3.4.9: "There shall be one or more functions to ensure safe vehicle
state in each step of software update operation."

Clause 8.3.1.2: Safe vehicle state is an example of metadata that must be
included in the software update package.

SAFE STATE ENFORCEMENT:

Safe state is enforced through:

1. PRE-UPDATE CHECKS: Verify safe state before initiating update
2. CONTINUOUS MONITORING: Monitor safe state during update
3. GUARD FUNCTIONS: Prevent transition to unsafe states during update
4. FAILURE RESPONSE: Respond appropriately if safe state is violated

SAFE VEHICLE STATE EXAMPLES IN PRACTICE:

EXAMPLE 1: ADAS UPDATE SAFE STATE

An OTA update for the ADAS system requires:

Download Phase:
- Vehicle can be in any state
- Background download over cellular or Wi-Fi

Installation Phase:
- Vehicle parked (detected via parking brake, transmission state, GPS)
- Engine off (to ensure ADAS sensors not active)
- Battery above 40% charge

Activation Phase:
- Same as installation
- User confirmation obtained
- ADAS functions disabled during activation

If any condition is violated (e.g., user opens door, shifts out of park),
the activation is paused or aborted.

EXAMPLE 2: BATTERY MANAGEMENT SYSTEM UPDATE SAFE STATE

An update for an electric vehicle's battery management system requires:

Download Phase:
- Any vehicle state

Installation Phase:
- Vehicle not charging
- High voltage contactors open
- Battery temperature between 10-35°C
- State of charge between 20-80%

Activation Phase:
- Same as installation
- Vehicle not connected to charger
- User confirmation obtained

If conditions change (e.g., user plugs in charger), the update is suspended.

The concept of skilled person recognizes that certain software update

CATEGORIES OF SKILLED PERSONS:

2. AUTHORIZED/CERTIFIED PERSONS:
   - Individuals with specific certifications
   - May be OEM-specific or third-party certified
   - Trained and authorized for specific operations
   - Access to restricted update procedures

3. SKILLED VEHICLE USERS:
   - Vehicle users with technical background
   - May have professional training
   - Can perform limited advanced operations
   - Still restricted from certain operations

SKILLED PERSON REQUIREMENTS:

ISO 24089 addresses skilled person requirements in:

1. Clause 8.3.1.8: "Necessary actions by vehicle user or skilled person

2. Clause 9.3.1.12: "Organization shall specify need for special equipment,
   skilled person or actions to complete software update operations in vehicle."
   - Campaign plan must identify skilled person requirements

3. Clause 9.3.2.13: "Software update campaign activities requiring special
equipment or training shall be performed by skilled person."
   - Execution must ensure skilled person is present when required

DETERMINING SKILLED PERSON REQUIREMENTS:

Organizations must assess when skilled persons are required:

1. SAFETY-CRITICAL UPDATES: Updates affecting safety systems typically
   require skilled person presence

2. COMPLEX PROCEDURES: Updates involving multiple steps, manual

3. SPECIAL EQUIPMENT: Updates requiring diagnostic tools, programming

TERM 3.1.15 - SOFTWARE

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Computer programs and associated data intended for installation on vehicles,
vehicle systems, or ECUs that may be dynamically written or modified during
execution.
Source: NIST SP 800-53, modified."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

This definition establishes the scope of what ISO 24089 considers "software"
in the context of software update engineering. The definition is intentionally
broad, encompassing both executable code and associated data.

SOFTWARE COMPONENTS:

1. EXECUTABLE CODE:
   - Application software
   - Operating systems
   - Bootloaders
   - Diagnostic software
   - Network protocol stacks

2. ASSOCIATED DATA:
   - Calibration data
   - Configuration parameters
   - Look-up tables
   - Machine learning models
   - Neural network weights
   - Localization data
   - Graphics assets

3. DYNAMIC ELEMENTS:
   - Data that can be modified during execution
   - Adaptive algorithms
   - Self-learning systems
   - Personalization data

SOFTWARE UPDATE SCOPE:

Software updates can affect any or all of these components:

1. CODE UPDATES:
   - Bug fixes
   - Feature additions
   - Performance improvements
   - Security patches

2. DATA UPDATES:
   - Calibration changes
   - Configuration updates
   - Map updates
   - Model updates

3. COMBINED UPDATES:
   - Code and data updated together
   - Dependencies between code and data managed

SOFTWARE CHARACTERISTICS FOR UPDATE:

ISO 24089's definition includes software "that may be dynamically written
or modified during execution," which has implications:

1. SELF-MODIFYING SOFTWARE:
   - Machine learning models that adapt
   - Adaptive calibration systems
   - Must be considered in update planning

2. RUNTIME MODIFICATION:
   - Software may change during operation
   - Updates must account for current state
   - Rollback may need to reset to baseline

SOFTWARE IN PRACTICE - EXAMPLES:

- Executable code (control algorithms)

- Configuration data (engine variant, market)

An update might modify:
- Control algorithms for improved emissions
- Calibration data for different fuel quality
- Configuration for new market requirements

EXAMPLE 2: AUTONOMOUS DRIVING SOFTWARE

Autonomous driving software includes:
- Executable code (perception, planning, control)
- Machine learning models (object detection, prediction)
- Configuration data (vehicle parameters, sensor calibration)

An update might modify:
- Perception algorithms
- Machine learning model weights
- Sensor fusion parameters

5. DOCUMENTATION:

   - Generate campaign reports

2. REACTIVE CAMPAIGNS:

3. OPPORTUNISTIC CAMPAIGNS:

9.3.2: Campaign Execution

TERM 3.1.17 - SOFTWARE UPDATE DISTRIBUTION METHOD

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Mechanism for delivery of a software update package during a campaign.
EXAMPLES: Wired (tool, USB flash drive), wireless (cellular, Wi-Fi), hardware
replacement."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Distribution methods define how software update packages are transferred
to vehicles. ISO 24089 recognizes multiple methods, allowing organizations
to select appropriate methods for different circumstances.

DISTRIBUTION METHOD TYPES:

1. WIRED METHODS:

   a) DIAGNOSTIC TOOL:
      - Professional diagnostic equipment
      - Connected to vehicle diagnostic port
      - Typically workshop-based
      - Reliable, high-speed transfer
      
   b) USB FLASH DRIVE:
      - USB storage device inserted in vehicle
      - User-initiated updates
      - Lower cost distribution
      - Applicable where OTA not available
      
   c) WIRELINE ETHERNET:
      - Direct Ethernet connection
      - High-speed transfer
      - Workshop or depot use

2. WIRELESS METHODS:

   a) CELLULAR (OTA):
      - Updates via cellular network (4G/5G)
      - Broad geographic reach
      - Can occur anywhere with coverage
      - May have data costs
      
   b) WI-FI (OTA):
      - Updates via Wi-Fi network
      - Higher bandwidth than cellular
      - Typically at home or office
      - No cellular data costs
      
   c) VEHICLE-TO-VEHICLE:
      - Updates distributed between vehicles
      - Emerging approach
      - Applicable in fleet environments

3. HARDWARE REPLACEMENT:

   a) ECU REPLACEMENT:
      - Physical replacement of ECU
      - Applicable for hardware changes
      - Workshop-based
      
   b) MODULE SWAP:
      - Swappable modules for updates
      - Less common
      - Specific use cases

METHOD SELECTION CRITERIA:

Organizations select distribution methods based on:

1. PACKAGE SIZE: Large packages may require high-bandwidth methods
2. CRITICALITY: Safety-critical may require workshop methods
3. REACH: OTA provides broader reach than workshop methods
4. RELIABILITY: Workshop methods more reliable than OTA
5. COST: OTA typically lower cost than workshop visits
6. SPEED: OTA can reach many vehicles simultaneously

DISTRIBUTION METHOD REQUIREMENTS IN ISO 24089:

Clause 6.3.4.4: "Infrastructure shall have one or more functions to support
software update distribution methods."

Clause 7.3.4.1: "There shall be one or more functions to support software
update distribution methods."

Clause 8.3.1.6: "Constraints on software update distribution methods of
software update package shall be identified."

Clause 9.3.1.6: "Organization shall determine which software update distribution
method(s) are used for each campaign."

DISTRIBUTION METHOD IN PRACTICE - EXAMPLES:

EXAMPLE 1: MULTI-METHOD CAMPAIGN

A campaign to update vehicle firmware uses multiple methods:

Primary: OTA via cellular and Wi-Fi
- Reaches 80% of target vehicles
- Cost-effective, convenient

Secondary: USB update for OTA-unavailable vehicles
- For vehicles without connectivity
- Distributed via mail or dealer

Fallback: Workshop update
- For complex cases
- Technician assistance required

EXAMPLE 2: WORKSHOP-ONLY CAMPAIGN

A safety-critical campaign uses workshop method only:

Distribution: Diagnostic tool at authorized dealerships
- Ensures technician oversight
- Enables verification
- Provides documentation
- Regulatory compliance

TERM 3.1.19 - SOFTWARE UPDATE OPERATION

EXACT DEFINITION:
"Steps involved in receipt, installation, and activation of software update
packages in a vehicle, vehicle systems, or ECUs."

COMPREHENSIVE EXPLANATION:

Software update operation defines the sequence of steps that occur on the
vehicle side when receiving and applying a software update. The standard
identifies three distinct steps: receipt, installation, and activation.

OPERATION STEPS:

1. RECEIPT (3.2.1):
   "Step in software update operation when a tool, vehicle, vehicle system,
   or ECU receives a software update package."
   
   Activities during receipt:
   - Package download from backend
   - Package transfer from external media
   - Package receipt from diagnostic tool
   - Storage of package in vehicle

2. INSTALLATION (3.2.2):
   "Step in software update operation when relevant parts of a software
   update package are written to a vehicle, vehicle system, or ECU but
   not yet activated."
   
   Activities during installation:
   - Package extraction and validation
   - Flash programming of ECUs
   - Configuration data updates
   - Installation verification

3. ACTIVATION (3.2.3):
   "Step in software update operation when relevant parts of an installed
   software update package become executable on a vehicle, vehicle system,
   or ECU."
   
   Activities during activation:
   - Switch to new software version
   - Configuration activation
   - System initialization
   - Function enablement

OPERATION SEPARATION:

The separation of these steps provides several benefits:

1. FLEXIBILITY: Steps can be performed at different times
   - Download during normal operation
   - Install when vehicle parked
   - Activate when user confirms

2. RELIABILITY: Each step can be verified independently
   - Verify download integrity
   - Verify installation success
   - Verify activation proper

3. ROLLBACK: Intermediate states enable recovery
   - Rollback before activation is simpler
   - Previous version still available until activation

4. STAGING: Enables staged deployment
   - Packages pre-positioned on vehicles
   - Activated when appropriate

OPERATION REQUIREMENTS IN ISO 24089:

Clause 7.3.4.2: "There shall be one or more functions to support software
update operations (receipt, installation, activation)."

Clause 9.3.2.5: "Necessary conditions to perform software update operation
shall be met at latest prior to activation."

OPERATION IN PRACTICE - EXAMPLES:

EXAMPLE 1: OTA UPDATE OPERATION SEQUENCE

Receipt:
- Day 1: Vehicle receives notification of available update
- Day 2: User initiates download
- Download completes, package stored in vehicle

Installation:
- Day 3: Vehicle parked, conditions checked
- Installation initiated
- Flash programming completes
- Installation verified

Activation:
- Day 3: User prompted to confirm activation
- User confirms
- System switches to new version
- New features active

EXAMPLE 2: DIAGNOSTIC TOOL UPDATE OPERATION

Receipt:
- Vehicle at workshop
- Technician connects diagnostic tool
- Package transferred to vehicle

Installation:
- Technician initiates update
- ECU programmed via diagnostic interface
- Installation verified

Activation:
- Automatic after installation verification
- ECU reboots with new software
- Technician performs verification test

SECTION 4.1: OVERVIEW AND OBJECTIVES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"4.1 OBJECTIVES
Ensure the following are performed:
a) Establishing organization-specific rules and processes for software update
   engineering
b) Adopting quality management, functional safety management and cybersecurity
   management for software update engineering
c) Instituting and maintaining a continuous improvement process
d) Establishing an information sharing policy
e) Conducting an organizational audit for process compliance"

DETAILED EXPLANATION OF OBJECTIVES:

The organizational level represents the foundation of ISO 24089 compliance.
Before any software update project can be undertaken, before infrastructure
can be developed, before any campaign can be executed, the organization must
establish the governance framework within which all software update engineering
activities occur. Clause 4 establishes this framework.

The five objectives listed in 4.1 form a comprehensive management system:

OBJECTIVE A: ORGANIZATION-SPECIFIC RULES AND PROCESSES

Every organization has unique characteristics that influence how it implements
software update engineering. Size, structure, geographic distribution, product
portfolio, customer base, regulatory environment, and existing management
systems all affect the appropriate approach. Objective (a) requires organizations
to establish rules and processes tailored to their specific context.

This objective recognizes that a one-size-fits-all approach is inappropriate.
A large OEM with thousands of employees, multiple vehicle platforms, and
millions of vehicles in the field will implement software update engineering
differently than a small Tier-2 supplier providing a specific software component.
Yet both must have defined rules and processes appropriate to their role.

The establishment of rules and processes includes:

1. POLICY DEFINITION: What are the organization's principles and commitments
   for software update engineering? What standards must be met? What quality
   levels are required? How are risks to be managed?

2. PROCESS DEFINITION: What activities must be performed? In what sequence?
With what inputs and outputs? With what verification requirements?

3. ROLE DEFINITION: Who is responsible for what? What authorities are granted?
What competencies are required? How are responsibilities assigned and tracked?

4. RESOURCE PROVISION: What resources (people, tools, infrastructure) are
   needed? How are they obtained and maintained? How is adequacy ensured?

OBJECTIVE B: MANAGEMENT SYSTEM ADOPTION

Software update engineering does not exist in isolation. It must integrate
with existing management systems for quality, functional safety, and
cybersecurity. Objective (b) requires adoption of these management systems.

Quality Management (ISO 9001, IATF 16949):
- Quality planning for software update activities
- Quality control for update processes
- Quality improvement through metrics and corrective actions
- Quality assurance through audits and assessments

Functional Safety Management (ISO 26262):
- Safety culture and competency requirements
- Safety life cycle processes
- Safety validation and confirmation measures
- Safety documentation and traceability

Cybersecurity Management (ISO/SAE 21434):
- Cybersecurity governance and organizational processes
- Cybersecurity risk management
- Cybersecurity incident response
- Cybersecurity monitoring and improvement

The integration of these management systems with software update engineering
requires careful planning. Organizations must identify where these systems
overlap, where they may conflict, and how to create a cohesive management
approach that addresses all requirements efficiently.

OBJECTIVE C: CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT

Software update engineering is an evolving discipline. Technologies change,
threats evolve, regulations develop, and organizational capabilities mature.
Objective (c) requires a continuous improvement process.

Continuous improvement in software update engineering encompasses:

1. PROCESS IMPROVEMENT: How can software update processes be made more
efficient, more effective, or more robust? What bottlenecks exist? What
redundancies can be eliminated?

2. TECHNICAL IMPROVEMENT: How can update technologies be upgraded? What
new tools and techniques can be adopted? How can update speed, reliability,
or security be enhanced?

3. ORGANIZATIONAL IMPROVEMENT: How can organizational structures be optimized?
What skills need development? What resources need expansion?

4. LESSONS LEARNED: What has gone well? What has gone poorly? What can
be learned from successes and failures? How can these lessons be captured
and applied?

OBJECTIVE D: INFORMATION SHARING POLICY

Software update engineering involves multiple stakeholders both within and
outside the organization. Objective (d) requires an information sharing policy.

Information sharing is essential because:

1. INTERNAL COORDINATION: Different departments (engineering, manufacturing,
service, quality) need to share information to coordinate software update
activities effectively.

2. SUPPLY CHAIN COORDINATION: Suppliers, subcontractors, and service providers
need appropriate information to fulfill their roles in software update
engineering.

3. REGULATORY COMPLIANCE: Regulatory authorities may require specific
information about software update capabilities and activities.

4. CUSTOMER COMMUNICATION: Vehicle users need information about software
updates affecting their vehicles.

The information sharing policy must address:
- What information is shared
- With whom it is shared
- When it is shared
- How it is shared
- What protections apply

OBJECTIVE E: ORGANIZATIONAL AUDIT

Compliance cannot be assumed; it must be verified. Objective (e) requires
organizational audits to verify process compliance.

Audits serve multiple purposes:

1. COMPLIANCE VERIFICATION: Do actual practices match defined processes? Are
requirements being met?

2. EFFECTIVENESS ASSESSMENT: Are processes achieving their intended outcomes?
Are objectives being met?

3. IMPROVEMENT IDENTIFICATION: What opportunities for improvement exist?
What issues need to be addressed?

4. EVIDENCE GENERATION: What evidence demonstrates compliance to regulators,
customers, and other stakeholders?

SECTION 4.2: GENERAL REQUIREMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"4.2 GENERAL
This clause covers organizational responsibility for governance so that software
update engineering processes conform to requirements. Governance includes:
- Compliance with required ISO standards
- Organizational activities: continuous improvement, information sharing,
  supporting processes
- Auditing requirements"

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

The general clause establishes that Clause 4 is about organizational governance.
Governance is the system by which an organization is directed and controlled.
In the context of software update engineering, governance ensures that:

1. APPROPRIATE DIRECTION IS SET: The organization defines its objectives,
strategies, and policies for software update engineering.

2. APPROPRIATE CONTROLS ARE IN PLACE: Processes, procedures, and controls
ensure that software update engineering activities are performed appropriately.

3. APPROPRIATE MONITORING OCCURS: The organization monitors performance,
compliance, and outcomes to ensure objectives are achieved.

4. APPROPRIATE ACCOUNTABILITY EXISTS: Roles, responsibilities, and authorities
are clear, and individuals are accountable for their areas of responsibility.

GOVERNANCE FOR SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING:

Governance for software update engineering must address several specific areas:

1. COMPLIANCE GOVERNANCE:
   - Identifying applicable requirements (ISO 24089, ISO 26262, ISO/SAE 21434,
     UN R156, regional regulations)
   - Ensuring processes are designed to meet requirements
   - Monitoring compliance through audits and assessments
   - Maintaining evidence of compliance

2. RISK GOVERNANCE:
   - Identifying risks associated with software update engineering
   - Establishing risk appetite and tolerance
   - Ensuring appropriate risk treatment
   - Monitoring risk status and emerging risks

3. RESOURCE GOVERNANCE:
   - Ensuring adequate resources are allocated
   - Managing resource utilization
   - Planning for future resource needs
   - Optimizing resource efficiency

4. PERFORMANCE GOVERNANCE:
   - Setting performance objectives
   - Monitoring performance metrics
   - Addressing performance gaps
   - Driving performance improvement

THREE PILLARS OF ORGANIZATIONAL GOVERNANCE:

ISO 24089 organizes governance into three pillars:

PILLAR 1: COMPLIANCE WITH ISO STANDARDS

The organization must comply with the normatively referenced standards:
- ISO 26262-6: Product development at the software level
- ISO 26262-8: Supporting processes
- ISO/SAE 21434: Cybersecurity engineering

This compliance is not optional; it is mandated by the normative references.
Organizations must demonstrate how they meet these requirements in the
context of software update engineering.

PILLAR 2: ORGANIZATIONAL ACTIVITIES

Beyond the normatively referenced standards, organizations must perform
specific organizational activities:
- Continuous improvement (4.3.2)
- Information sharing (4.3.3)
- Supporting processes (4.3.4)

These activities are essential for maintaining effective software update
engineering capability over time.

PILLAR 3: AUDITING

Auditing provides the verification function that ensures governance is
effective. Without auditing, governance may become a paper exercise rather
than a real practice.

SECTION 4.3.1: GOVERNANCE
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"4.3.1.1 SHALL: If the organization performs software update engineering

4.3.1.2 SHALL: Organization shall establish, document, and maintain rules

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

These three requirements establish the fundamental governance framework for
software update engineering. Let us examine each in detail.

REQUIREMENT 4.3.1.1 - APPLICABILITY:

This requirement establishes a clear trigger for the standard's applicability:
if the organization performs software update engineering activities, the
standard applies. There is no size threshold, no activity threshold, no
exemption criteria. The presence of software update engineering activities
triggers applicability.

What constitutes "performing software update engineering activities"? Based
on the definition of software update engineering (planning, development,
and deployment of software update packages), activities include:

- Planning activities:
  - Defining software update strategies
  - Planning software update projects
  - Planning software update campaigns
  - Assessing risks for software updates

- Development activities:
  - Developing software for updateable systems
  - Developing update infrastructure
  - Developing update packages
  - Developing update mechanisms in vehicles

- Deployment activities:
  - Executing software update campaigns
  - Distributing software update packages
  - Monitoring software update operations
  - Completing software update campaigns

The broad scope means that many organizations in the automotive industry
fall within the standard's applicability, including:

- OEMs who design vehicles with update capability and execute campaigns
- Tier-1 suppliers who develop updateable ECUs and provide software updates
- Tier-2 suppliers who develop software components for updateable systems
- Infrastructure providers who operate backend systems for OTA updates
- Service providers who perform software updates at workshops
- Fleet operators who coordinate updates across their vehicle fleets

PRACTICAL EXAMPLES OF APPLICABILITY:

EXAMPLE 1: OEM WITH OTA CAPABILITY

A global automotive OEM develops vehicles with over-the-air update capability.
The OEM:
- Designs vehicles to be updateable
- Operates backend infrastructure for OTA distribution
- Develops software update packages
- Plans and executes software update campaigns

This organization clearly performs software update engineering activities
and must comply with ISO 24089 in full.

EXAMPLE 2: TIER-1 SUPPLIER OF UPDATEABLE ECUs

A Tier-1 supplier develops engine control modules with flash update capability.
The supplier:
- Designs ECUs to be updateable
- Provides bootloader and update mechanism
- Develops software updates for the ECUs
- Coordinates with OEMs for update integration

This organization performs software update engineering activities and must
comply with relevant portions of ISO 24089.

EXAMPLE 3: SOFTWARE DEVELOPMENT TOOL VENDOR

A company provides development tools used in automotive software development.
They do not:
- Design updateable systems
- Develop update infrastructure
- Create update packages
- Execute update campaigns

This organization does not perform software update engineering activities
and ISO 24089 does not directly apply. However, they may need to understand
the standard to support customers who are subject to it.

REQUIREMENT 4.3.1.2 - RULES AND PROCESSES:

This requirement mandates that organizations establish, document, and
maintain rules and processes for software update engineering. Each of
these three elements has specific meaning:

ESTABLISH: Create and define the rules and processes
- Analyze requirements from ISO 24089 and other applicable standards
- Define processes to meet requirements
- Define rules governing process execution
- Obtain appropriate management approval

DOCUMENT: Record the rules and processes in a tangible form
- Write process descriptions, procedures, work instructions
- Create process flowcharts, decision trees
- Document roles, responsibilities, authorities
- Create templates, forms, checklists
- Maintain documentation in accessible location

MAINTAIN: Keep the rules and processes current and effective
- Review processes periodically
- Update processes when requirements change
- Update processes when improvements are identified
- Ensure documentation remains accessible and controlled

The rules and processes must achieve three purposes:

PURPOSE 1: ENABLE IMPLEMENTATION OF DOCUMENT REQUIREMENTS

Rules and processes must provide the framework that makes compliance possible.
Without defined processes, individuals would need to figure out how to
meet requirements on their own, leading to inconsistency and gaps.

For example, the requirement in Clause 8 to verify and validate software
update packages is enabled by:
- A defined V&V process specifying what activities are required
- Procedures for each V&V activity
- Templates for V&V documentation
- Checklists for V&V completeness
- Criteria for V&V acceptance

PURPOSE 2: SUPPORT EXECUTION WITH RESOURCES AND RESPONSIBILITIES

Rules and processes must provide the operational guidance that makes
execution possible:
- Resource requirements are specified (who, what, how many)
- Responsibilities are assigned (who does what)
- Authority is delegated (who can make what decisions)
- Interfaces are defined (how different parties interact)

For example, a process for software update campaign planning might specify:
- Campaign manager has overall responsibility
- Engineering provides technical input
- Quality reviews V&V evidence
- Security reviews cybersecurity measures
- Service operations assesses support requirements
- Resources from each department are identified
- Timeline and dependencies are defined

PURPOSE 3: CONFIRM CONFORMANCE WITH REQUIREMENTS

Rules and processes must enable verification that requirements are met:
- Criteria for conformance are defined
- Evidence requirements are specified
- Verification activities are built into processes
- Nonconformance handling is defined
- Improvement mechanisms are included

SCOPE OF RULES AND PROCESSES:

The note clarifies that rules and processes must cover:

1. AFFECTED VEHICLE SYSTEMS: Any vehicle system that is updateable or
   that may be affected by software updates must be within scope.

2. INFRASTRUCTURE: All infrastructure used for software update engineering
   must be within scope, including backend systems, workshop tools, and
   manual processes.

DETAILED REQUIREMENTS FOR RULES AND PROCESSES:

Organizations typically establish rules and processes at multiple levels:

LEVEL 1: POLICIES

Policies establish principles and commitments at the highest level:

SOFTWARE UPDATE POLICY EXAMPLE:

"[Organization Name] is committed to software update engineering excellence.
We will:

1. Develop vehicles and vehicle systems that can be safely and securely
   updated throughout their operational life.

2. Maintain infrastructure that supports reliable and secure software
   update distribution.

3. Develop software update packages that meet all functional safety and
   cybersecurity requirements.

4. Execute software update campaigns that maintain vehicle safety and
   customer satisfaction.

5. Continuously improve our software update engineering capabilities.

6. Comply with all applicable requirements including ISO 24089, ISO 26262,
   ISO/SAE 21434, and relevant regulations.

This policy applies to all activities related to software update engineering
performed by or for [Organization Name]."

LEVEL 2: PROCESSES

Processes define the high-level activities required:

SOFTWARE UPDATE PACKAGE DEVELOPMENT PROCESS EXAMPLE:

"Process: Software Update Package Development

Purpose: Define the activities required to develop, verify, and approve
software update packages for deployment.

Scope: All software update packages for [Organization Name] products.

Activities:
1. Target Identification - Identify target vehicles/systems/ECUs
2. Content Selection - Select software and metadata for package
3. Compatibility Analysis - Analyze compatibility requirements
4. Dependency Analysis - Identify and analyze dependencies
5. Resource Identification - Identify required in-vehicle resources
6. Package Assembly - Assemble the update package
7. Verification - Verify package contents and integrity
8. Validation - Validate package functionality
9. Approval - Obtain approval for release

Entry Criteria: Approved software release, defined update requirements
Exit Criteria: Approved software update package ready for campaign
Key Interfaces: Software development, Quality, Security, Infrastructure"

REQUIREMENT 4.3.1.3 - INTEGRATION WITH ISO STANDARDS:

This requirement mandates that software update engineering activities be
performed in accordance with three normatively referenced standards.

INTEGRATION WITH ISO/SAE 21434 (CYBERSECURITY ENGINEERING):

Software update engineering must incorporate cybersecurity engineering
practices from ISO/SAE 21434. Key integrations include:

1. GOVERNANCE INTEGRATION:
   - Cybersecurity governance requirements apply to software update engineering
   - Cybersecurity culture must extend to update activities
   - Cybersecurity competency requirements apply to update personnel

2. RISK MANAGEMENT INTEGRATION:
   - Cybersecurity risks must be assessed for update systems and processes
   - Threat scenarios for update systems must be analyzed
   - Risk treatment measures must be implemented

3. CONTINUOUS MONITORING:
   - Update infrastructure must be monitored for cybersecurity incidents
   - Update mechanisms must be monitored for vulnerabilities
   - Incident response must cover update-related incidents

4. POST-PRODUCTION MANAGEMENT:
   - Cybersecurity monitoring extends to update capability
   - Vulnerabilities in update systems must be addressed
   - Security patches may require update mechanism updates

INTEGRATION WITH ISO 26262-6 (SOFTWARE PRODUCT DEVELOPMENT):

Software update engineering must incorporate functional safety software
development practices from ISO 26262-6 when updates affect safety-critical
software. Key integrations include:

1. SAFETY REQUIREMENTS:
   - Updated software must satisfy safety requirements
   - Safety requirements must be traceable through update process
   - Changes to safety-critical software require safety analysis

2. SOFTWARE DESIGN:
   - Updated software design must meet ASIL requirements
   - Design verification must include safety considerations
   - Safety mechanisms must be preserved through updates

3. VERIFICATION:
   - Verification of updated software must meet ASIL requirements
   - Testing must demonstrate continued safety
   - Regression testing for safety functions required

4. CONFIGURATION MANAGEMENT:
   - Safety-critical configuration items must be managed through updates
   - Traceability between requirements and implementation must be maintained
   - Version control for safety-critical software must be maintained

INTEGRATION WITH ISO 26262-8 (SUPPORTING PROCESSES):

Software update engineering must incorporate supporting processes from
ISO 26262-8. Key integrations include:

1. CHANGE MANAGEMENT:
   - Changes through software updates must follow change management process
   - Impact assessment must include safety implications
   - Change approval must consider safety and security

2. CONFIGURATION MANAGEMENT:
   - Configuration items must be managed through update lifecycle
   - Configuration baseline must be maintained
   - Configuration status accounting must reflect updates

3. VERIFICATION:
   - Verification planning must address update scenarios
   - Verification methods must be appropriate for changes
   - Verification results must be documented

Phase 5 - Validation:
- Validate cybersecurity measures

Phase 6 - Operations:
- Monitor for security incidents
- Maintain security patches
- Respond to vulnerabilities

EXAMPLE 2: FUNCTIONAL SAFETY INTEGRATION FOR BRAKE ECU UPDATE

A supplier developing a brake ECU software update integrates ISO 26262-6

SECTION 4.3.2: CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"4.3.2.1 SHALL: Organization shall establish, perform and maintain a
continuous improvement process for software update engineering activities.

Examples: Evaluating/applying lessons learned, improvements from previous/
similar projects, KPIs for continuous improvement.

4.3.2.2 SHALL: Organization shall establish, perform and maintain a process
to verify that after any change to software update engineering processes,
the processes still meet document requirements."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

Continuous improvement is a fundamental principle of modern management systems.
ISO 24089 requires that organizations establish processes for continuous
improvement of software update engineering activities. The requirements
distinguish between improving activities (4.3.2.1) and maintaining compliance
when changes occur (4.3.2.2).

REQUIREMENT 4.3.2.1 - CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT PROCESS:

The continuous improvement process must include:

1. IMPROVEMENT IDENTIFICATION:
   How does the organization identify opportunities for improvement?
   
   a) PROACTIVE IDENTIFICATION:
      - Regular process reviews
      - Benchmarking against industry practices
      - Technology trend monitoring
      - Innovation initiatives
   
   b) REACTIVE IDENTIFICATION:
      - Problem analysis
      - Customer feedback
      - Audit findings
      - Incident investigation

2. IMPROVEMENT EVALUATION:
   How does the organization evaluate improvement opportunities?
   
   - Benefit analysis (what will improve?)
   - Cost analysis (what will it require?)
   - Risk analysis (what could go wrong?)
   - Feasibility analysis (can we implement it?)
   - Priority analysis (when should we do it?)

3. IMPROVEMENT IMPLEMENTATION:
   How does the organization implement improvements?
   
   - Planning improvement implementation
   - Allocating resources
   - Executing implementation
   - Monitoring progress
   - Completing implementation

4. IMPROVEMENT VERIFICATION:
   How does the organization verify improvements are effective?
   
   - Defining success criteria
   - Measuring improvement results
   - Comparing to baseline
   - Confirming benefits achieved

CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT MECHANISMS:

The standard provides examples of continuous improvement mechanisms:

1. LESSONS LEARNED:
   Capturing and applying lessons from completed activities:
   
   a) LESSON CAPTURE:
      - Post-project reviews
      - Post-campaign analysis
      - Incident investigation
      - Audit findings review
   
   b) LESSON DOCUMENTATION:
      - What happened?
      - Why did it happen?
      - What should be done differently?
      - What should be repeated?
   
   c) LESSON APPLICATION:
      - Process updates
      - Training updates
      - Tool improvements
      - Resource allocation changes

2. IMPROVEMENTS FROM PREVIOUS/SIMILAR PROJECTS:
   Applying learning across projects:
   
   - Project retrospectives
   - Cross-project knowledge sharing
   - Best practice identification
   - Common issue analysis

3. KEY PERFORMANCE INDICATORS:
   Measuring performance to drive improvement:
   
   a) PROCESS KPIs:
      - Process compliance rate
      - Process cycle time
      - Process defect rate
      - Process efficiency metrics
   
   b) OUTCOME KPIs:
      - Update success rate
      - Update completion time
      - Customer satisfaction with updates
      - Safety/security incident rate

ESTABLISHING KPIs FOR SOFTWARE UPDATE ENGINEERING:

Organizations should establish KPIs appropriate to their software update
engineering activities. Examples include:

PROCESS EFFECTIVENESS KPIs:

1. CAMPAIGN SUCCESS RATE
   Formula: (Successful updates / Total attempted updates) x 100%
   Target: > 98%
   Improvement: Investigate failures, implement corrective actions

2. AVERAGE UPDATE DURATION
   Formula: Sum of update durations / Number of updates
   Target: Within defined threshold
   Improvement: Optimize package size, improve network bandwidth

3. PACKAGE V&V DEFECT ESCAPE RATE
   Formula: (Defects found post-release / Total defects) x 100%
   Target: < 5%
   Improvement: Enhance V&V processes, add test coverage

4. PROCESS COMPLIANCE SCORE
   Formula: (Compliant process executions / Total process executions) x 100%
   Target: 100%
   Improvement: Training, process simplification, tool support

EFFICIENCY KPIs:

1. CAMPAIGN CYCLE TIME
   Formula: Time from campaign initiation to completion
   Target: Within defined threshold
   Improvement: Process optimization, automation, parallelization

2. PACKAGE DEVELOPMENT TIME
   Formula: Time from software release to package approval
   Target: Within defined threshold
   Improvement: Automation, template reuse, tool integration

3. RESOURCE UTILIZATION
   Formula: Actual resource usage / Planned resource usage
   Target: 80-100%
   Improvement: Better planning, resource optimization

OUTCOME KPIs:

1. CUSTOMER SATISFACTION WITH UPDATES
   Formula: Survey score or feedback analysis
   Target: > 4.0 / 5.0
   Improvement: Communication, update timing, feature value

2. SAFETY INCIDENT RATE
   Formula: Safety incidents related to updates / Total updates
   Target: 0
   Improvement: Enhanced safety analysis, testing, verification

3. SECURITY INCIDENT RATE
   Formula: Security incidents related to updates / Total updates
   Target: 0
   Improvement: Enhanced security analysis, testing, monitoring

REQUIREMENT 4.3.2.2 - PROCESS CHANGE VERIFICATION:

This requirement addresses a specific risk: when processes are changed,
the organization must verify that the changed processes still meet ISO 24089
requirements. This prevents improvements from inadvertently creating
compliance gaps.

The process change verification process includes:

1. CHANGE IDENTIFICATION:
   - What process is being changed?
   - What is the nature of the change?
   - Why is the change being made?

2. REQUIREMENTS MAPPING:
   - What ISO 24089 requirements does the process address?
   - What other standards requirements does the process address?
   - How does the change affect requirement coverage?

3. COMPLIANCE VERIFICATION:
   - Does the changed process still meet requirements?
   - Have any requirements become uncovered?
   - Is new evidence needed for compliance demonstration?

4. DOCUMENTATION UPDATE:
   - Update process documentation
   - Update compliance mapping
   - Update evidence requirements

5. IMPLEMENTATION:
   - Implement changed process
   - Monitor for issues
   - Verify compliance in practice

EXAMPLE OF PROCESS CHANGE VERIFICATION:

SCENARIO: An organization wants to streamline its software update package
approval process by reducing the number of required sign-offs from five
to three.

CHANGE IDENTIFICATION:
- Process being changed: Package approval process
- Nature of change: Reduce approval signatures from 5 to 3
- Reason: Improve cycle time, reduce bottleneck

REQUIREMENTS MAPPING:
- ISO 24089 8.3.4.1 requires: "Software update package shall be approved
  for release based on verification and validation."
- Current process: 5 sign-offs ensure comprehensive review
- Changed process: 3 sign-offs with defined competency requirements

COMPLIANCE VERIFICATION:
- Does changed process still ensure approval based on V&V?
  Yes, if sign-offs include appropriate competency coverage
- What competencies must the 3 signers have?
  Technical authority, Quality assurance, Security
- Is the defined competency coverage adequate?
  Yes, covers technical quality and security aspects

DOCUMENTATION UPDATE:
- Update approval process procedure
- Define competency requirements for each role
- Update approval form template
- Update compliance matrix

IMPLEMENTATION:
- Communicate change to stakeholders
- Implement new approval process
- Monitor first approvals under new process
- Verify compliance in practice

SECTION 4.3.5: AUDITING
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"4.3.5.1 SHALL: An audit shall be performed to determine that organizational
processes for software update engineering achieve the objectives of this clause.

Notes: Audit can be included in/combined with quality management system audit.
Audit can be performed by internal or external organization.
To ensure organizational processes remain appropriate, audit can be performed
periodically.
In distributed development, audit rights can be included in contract."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

Auditing provides the verification function for organizational processes.
Without auditing, an organization cannot demonstrate that its processes
are effective and compliant.

AUDIT PURPOSE:

The audit determines whether organizational processes for software update
engineering achieve the objectives of Clause 4. These objectives are:

1. Organization-specific rules and processes are established
2. Management systems are adopted (quality, safety, cybersecurity)
3. Continuous improvement process is in place
4. Information sharing policy is established
5. Organizational audit is conducted

AUDIT SCOPE:

The audit must cover all aspects of organizational governance:

1. GOVERNANCE AUDIT:
   - Are rules and processes established, documented, and maintained?
   - Do rules and processes enable requirement implementation?
   - Are resources and responsibilities appropriately assigned?
   - Is conformance confirmation possible?

2. MANAGEMENT SYSTEM AUDIT:
   - Is ISO/SAE 21434 implemented for cybersecurity?
   - Is ISO 26262-6 implemented for functional safety software?
   - Is ISO 26262-8 implemented for supporting processes?

3. CONTINUOUS IMPROVEMENT AUDIT:
   - Is continuous improvement process established?
   - Are improvements being identified and implemented?
   - Is process change verification performed?

4. INFORMATION SHARING AUDIT:
   - Is information sharing policy established?
   - Is policy being followed?
   - Are information protections adequate?

5. SUPPORTING PROCESS AUDIT:
   - Is document management process effective?
   - Is requirements management process effective?
   - Are privacy considerations addressed?
   - Is configuration management process effective?
   - Is quality management process effective?
   - Is change management process effective?

AUDIT APPROACHES:

The standard notes several approaches to auditing:

1. INTEGRATED AUDIT:
   The software update engineering audit can be integrated with the
   organization's quality management system audit. This provides:
   - Efficiency through combined activities
   - Consistency with existing audit processes
   - Reduced audit burden

SECTION 5.1: OVERVIEW AND OBJECTIVES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"5.1 OBJECTIVES
Ensure the following are performed:
a) Planning for software update project including assigning roles and
   responsibilities
b) Managing and storing information regarding a software update project
c) Providing justifications for any tailoring of a software update project
d) Confirming interoperability of infrastructure and vehicle functions for
   a project
e) Preserving integrity of software, metadata or software update packages
   or both"

DETAILED EXPLANATION OF OBJECTIVES:

Clause 5 addresses project-level requirements for software update engineering.
While Clause 4 established organizational governance, Clause 5 focuses on how
individual software update projects are planned and managed. Each software
update initiative - whether developing new update capability, adapting
existing systems, or executing campaigns - constitutes a project that must
be properly managed.

OBJECTIVE A: PROJECT PLANNING

Planning is the foundation of successful project execution. Without proper
planning, projects suffer from unclear objectives, inadequate resources,
missed deadlines, and quality issues. Objective (a) requires comprehensive
project planning including role and responsibility assignment.

Project planning for software update engineering must address:

1. SCOPE PLANNING:
   - What is the project trying to achieve?
   - What vehicles, systems, or ECUs are affected?
   - What functionality is being developed or modified?
   - What are the boundaries of the project?

2. SCHEDULE PLANNING:
   - What are the project milestones?
   - What activities must be performed?
   - What are the dependencies between activities?
   - What is the critical path?

3. RESOURCE PLANNING:
   - What personnel are required?
   - What skills and competencies are needed?
   - What tools and equipment are required?
   - What budget is required?

4. RISK PLANNING:
   - What risks could affect the project?
   - How likely are these risks?
   - What would be their impact?
   - How will risks be mitigated?

5. QUALITY PLANNING:
   - What quality objectives must be met?
   - How will quality be verified?
   - What standards and processes apply?
   - What documentation is required?

6. COMMUNICATION PLANNING:
   - Who needs to be informed about the project?
   - What information do they need?
   - How will information be communicated?
   - How often will communication occur?

OBJECTIVE B: INFORMATION MANAGEMENT

Projects generate and consume significant amounts of information. Objective
(b) requires managing and storing information properly. This includes:

1. PROJECT DOCUMENTATION:
   - Project plan
   - Requirements documents
   - Design documents
   - Test documents
   - Approval records

2. TECHNICAL INFORMATION:
   - Software specifications
   - Interface specifications
   - Configuration information
   - Test results

3. MANAGEMENT INFORMATION:
   - Progress reports
   - Risk registers
   - Change requests
   - Decision records

4. COMPLIANCE INFORMATION:
   - Evidence of process compliance
   - Evidence of requirement satisfaction
   - Audit records
   - Approvals and sign-offs

OBJECTIVE C: TAILORING JUSTIFICATION

The standard recognizes that not all projects are the same. Different
projects may have different requirements, risks, and constraints. Tailoring
allows organizations to adapt the standard's requirements to their specific
context, but objective (c) requires justification for any tailoring.

Tailoring may be appropriate when:

1. PROJECT COMPLEXITY: Simpler projects may not require all activities
   defined for complex projects.

2. ORGANIZATIONAL ROLE: Different roles in the supply chain may have
   different requirements.

3. EXISTING CAPABILITIES: Projects building on existing capabilities may
   require less development effort.

4. REGULATORY CONTEXT: Different markets may have different regulatory
   requirements.

The justification must explain:
- What requirements are being tailored
- Why tailoring is appropriate
- How objectives will still be achieved
- What alternative measures are implemented

OBJECTIVE D: INTEROPERABILITY CONFIRMATION

Software update engineering involves multiple systems that must work together.
Infrastructure systems must communicate with vehicle systems. Vehicle systems
must interoperate with each other. Objective (d) requires confirming that
interoperability is achieved.

Interoperability must be confirmed between:

1. INFRASTRUCTURE AND VEHICLE:
   - Can the backend distribute packages to the vehicle?
   - Can the vehicle receive packages from the backend?
   - Can the vehicle report status to the backend?
   - Are communication protocols compatible?

2. VEHICLE SYSTEMS:
   - Can ECUs receive updates from the update manager?
   - Can the update manager coordinate multi-ECU updates?
   - Are vehicle networks properly configured?
   - Are dependencies properly managed?

3. ORGANIZATIONAL INTERFACES:
   - Can suppliers provide packages to OEMs?
   - Can information flow between organizations?
   - Are handoffs properly defined?

OBJECTIVE E: INTEGRITY PRESERVATION

Software update packages contain valuable and sensitive content that must
be protected from unauthorized modification. Objective (e) requires preserving
integrity of software, metadata, and packages.

Integrity must be preserved:

1. WITHIN ORGANIZATIONS:
   - During package development
   - During package storage
   - During internal distribution

2. BETWEEN ORGANIZATIONS:
   - During transfer to suppliers
   - During transfer from suppliers
   - During transfer to service providers

3. FROM ORGANIZATION TO VEHICLE:
   - During distribution to vehicles
   - During storage on vehicles
   - During installation

4. WITHIN VEHICLES:
   - During transfer between ECUs
   - During installation to target ECU
   - During activation

SECTION 5.2: GENERAL REQUIREMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"5.2 GENERAL
This clause covers organizational requirements for software update projects
including planning for software update projects, and managing information
related to such projects. Requirements on tailoring software update projects
and interoperability between parts of software update projects are included."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

Clause 5 operates at the project level, bridging organizational governance
(Clause 4) with technical implementation (Clauses 6-9). The general statement
highlights four key areas:

1. PROJECT PLANNING: How projects are planned and initiated
2. INFORMATION MANAGEMENT: How project information is managed
3. TAILORING: How the standard is adapted to project needs
4. INTEROPERABILITY: How project components work together

PROJECT CONTEXT:

Understanding Clause 5 requires understanding what constitutes a software
update project. A software update project is "set of software update engineering
activities for one or more targets." Examples include:

1. INFRASTRUCTURE DEVELOPMENT PROJECT:
   Developing or upgrading backend systems for OTA capability
   - Scope: Build OTA distribution capability
   - Duration: Months to years
   - Activities: Requirements, design, implementation, testing, deployment

2. VEHICLE CAPABILITY DEVELOPMENT PROJECT:
   Developing update capability for a new vehicle platform
   - Scope: Enable vehicle to receive and process updates
   - Duration: Aligned with vehicle development timeline
   - Activities: Requirements, design, implementation, integration, validation

3. UPDATE PACKAGE DEVELOPMENT PROJECT:
   Developing software update packages for a specific purpose
   - Scope: Create packages for identified targets
   - Duration: Weeks to months
   - Activities: Package assembly, V&V, approval

4. CAMPAIGN EXECUTION PROJECT:
   Planning and executing a software update campaign
   - Scope: Distribute packages to target vehicles
   - Duration: Weeks to months
   - Activities: Campaign planning, execution, monitoring, completion

PROJECT HIERARCHY:

Projects may exist at different levels:

LEVEL 1 - PROGRAM: A large initiative encompassing multiple projects
Example: Developing OTA capability for a new vehicle line

LEVEL 2 - PROJECT: A specific initiative within a program
Example: Developing the vehicle update manager component

LEVEL 3 - SUB-PROJECT: A component of a larger project
Example: Developing the package download function within the update manager

LEVEL 4 - CAMPAIGN: An operational activity within ongoing capability
Example: Distributing a specific update to vehicles

ISO 24089 Clause 5 applies at all these levels, with requirements scaled
appropriately to the scope and complexity of each project.

SECTION 5.3.2: TAILORING AND RATIONALE
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"5.3.2.1 A software update project may be tailored.

Examples: Identifies applicable content of ISO 26262-6, ISO 26262-8 and
ISO/SAE 21434. Example 2: Body builder tailors to conform with functional
safety standards (ISO 13849 or IEC 61508).

5.3.2.2 SHALL: If software update project is tailored, then a rationale
shall be provided as to how tailored activities achieve applicable objectives.

Notes: Activity is tailored if omitted or performed differently compared to
description. Software update engineering activities performed by another
entity in supply chain are considered distributed activities rather than
tailored activities. Can consult with suppliers on applicable clauses to
supplier's work."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

REQUIREMENT 5.3.2.1 - TAILORING PERMISSION:

The standard permits tailoring, which means organizations may adapt or
omit activities based on their specific circumstances. This permission
recognizes that:

1. NOT ALL PROJECTS ARE THE SAME: Different projects have different
   complexity, risk, and scope.

2. NOT ALL ORGANIZATIONS ARE THE SAME: Different organizations have
   different roles, capabilities, and constraints.

3. PRESCRIPTIVE REQUIREMENTS MAY NOT FIT: Rigid requirements may be
   inappropriate for some contexts.

WHAT CAN BE TAILOd:

Tailoring can affect:

1. ACTIVITIES: Certain activities may be omitted or performed differently
2. METHODS: Different methods may be used to achieve objectives
3. DOCUMENTATION: Documentation may be adapted to context
4. VERIFICATION: Verification approaches may be adapted

TAILORING VS. DISTRIBUTION:

The standard makes an important distinction:

TAILORING: An activity is performed differently or omitted by the
organization. The organization is still responsible for the outcome.

DISTRIBUTION: An activity is performed by another entity in the supply
chain. The activity is not omitted, just performed elsewhere.

This distinction matters because:

- Tailoring requires justification for how objectives are still met
- Distribution requires coordination with the entity performing the activity
- Both require ensuring that overall objectives are achieved

TAILORING EXAMPLES:

EXAMPLE 1: TIER-2 SUPPLIER TAILORING

A Tier-2 supplier provides a software component to a Tier-1 supplier.
The Tier-2 supplier's project scope is narrower than the full software
update engineering lifecycle.

Tailoring:
- The Tier-2 supplier does not execute vehicle-level validation
  (performed by OEM)
- The Tier-2 supplier does not execute campaigns
  (performed by OEM)
- The Tier-2 supplier focuses on component-level activities

Justification:
- Vehicle-level activities are outside Tier-2 scope
- These activities will be performed by OEM
- Component-level activities ensure component is suitable for integration

EXAMPLE 2: BODY BUILDER TAILORING

A body builder modifies a base vehicle to create a specialized vehicle
(e.g., ambulance, fire truck). The body builder's software update
engineering context differs from the base vehicle OEM.

Tailoring:
- The body builder applies ISO 13849 or IEC 61508 instead of ISO 26262
  for functional safety (different regulatory context)
- The body builder may have different cybersecurity requirements

Justification:
- ISO 13849/IEC 61508 are more appropriate for the body builder's context
- These standards achieve equivalent safety objectives
- Coordination with base vehicle OEM ensures interoperability

REQUIREMENT 5.3.2.2 - TAILORING RATIONALE:

When tailoring occurs, a rationale must be provided explaining how
objectives are still achieved. This requirement prevents arbitrary
omission of important activities.

RATIONALE REQUIREMENTS:

The rationale must:

1. IDENTIFY TAILORED ACTIVITIES: What is being omitted or done differently?

2. EXPLAIN TAILORING REASON: Why is tailoring appropriate?

3. DESCRIBE ALTERNATIVE APPROACH: How will objectives be achieved?

4. ASSESS IMPACT: What are the implications of tailoring?

5. CONFIRM OBJECTIVE ACHIEVEMENT: How will applicable objectives still
   be met?

RATIONALE DOCUMENTATION:

The rationale should be documented with:

1. PROJECT IDENTIFICATION: Which project is being tailored?

2. TAILORED REQUIREMENTS: Which requirements are tailored?

3. TAILORING DESCRIPTION: How are requirements tailored?

4. JUSTIFICATION: Why is tailoring appropriate?

5. RISK ASSESSMENT: What risks does tailoring introduce?

6. MITIGATION: How are risks mitigated?

7. APPROVAL: Who approved the tailoring?

SECTION 5.3.3: INTEROPERABILITY
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"5.3.3.1 SHALL: Organization shall establish, implement and maintain a
process to confirm interoperability of functions developed in accordance
with requirements from Clause 6 and Clause 7.

Note: Important to confirm interoperability between infrastructure and
vehicle system for successful software update campaign."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

Interoperability confirmation ensures that different components of the
software update engineering ecosystem work together correctly. This
requirement addresses the integration challenge between infrastructure
(Clause 6) and vehicle systems (Clause 7).

WHY INTEROPERABILITY MATTERS:

Software update engineering involves multiple systems developed by multiple
organizations. Without interoperability confirmation, integration issues
may not be discovered until campaign execution, when failures affect real
vehicles. Interoperability confirmation:

1. PREVENTS INTEGRATION FAILURES: Identifies issues before campaigns
2. REDUCES CAMPAIGN RISK: Ensures systems work together
3. ENABLES EARLY CORRECTION: Issues found earlier are cheaper to fix
4. BUILDS CONFIDENCE: Stakeholders trust the update capability

INTEROPERABILITY DOMAINS:

1. INFRASTRUCTURE-TO-VEHICLE INTEROPERABILITY:
   - Can backend distribute packages to vehicles?
   - Can vehicles receive packages from backend?
   - Are communication protocols compatible?
   - Are security mechanisms compatible?
   - Can vehicles report status to backend?

2. INFRASTRUCTURE-TO-INFRASTRUCTURE INTEROPERABILITY:
   - Can campaign management system control package repository?
   - Can monitoring systems receive data from backend?
   - Can security infrastructure support backend operations?

3. VEHICLE-TO-VEHICLE INTEROPERABILITY:
   - Can update manager coordinate with ECUs?
   - Can gateway route update traffic?
   - Can ECUs receive updates from update manager?

4. ORGANIZATION-TO-ORGANIZATION INTEROPERABILITY:
   - Can suppliers provide packages to OEMs?
   - Can OEMs integrate supplier packages?
   - Can service providers access required systems?

INTEROPERABILITY CONFIRMATION PROCESS:

The process to confirm interoperability should include:

1. INTEROPERABILITY REQUIREMENTS:
   - Define what interoperability is required
   - Specify interfaces and protocols
   - Define compatibility requirements

2. INTEROPERABILITY TESTING:
   - Test infrastructure-to-vehicle communication
   - Test package distribution flow
   - Test status reporting flow
   - Test security mechanisms

3. INTEROPERABILITY VERIFICATION:
   - Verify interfaces meet specifications
   - Verify protocols work correctly
   - Verify error handling
   - Verify recovery mechanisms

4. INTEROPERABILITY DOCUMENTATION:
   - Document interface specifications
   - Document test results
   - Document known limitations
   - Document workarounds

INTEROPERABILITY TEST APPROACHES:

1. LABORATORY TESTING:
   - Test infrastructure with representative vehicle systems
   - Use test benches and development vehicles
   - Simulate various scenarios

2. PILOT TESTING:
   - Test with limited number of production vehicles
   - Monitor for issues
   - Validate in real-world conditions

3. FIELD TRIALS:
   - Test with broader vehicle population
   - Monitor for integration issues
   - Validate across different conditions

INTEROPERABILITY CONFIRMATION EXAMPLE:

PROJECT: OTA Infrastructure Integration with Model Year 2025 Vehicles

INTEROPERABILITY REQUIREMENTS:
1. Backend shall distribute packages via cellular network
2. Vehicles shall receive packages via cellular module
3. Vehicles shall authenticate packages using OEM certificates
4. Vehicles shall report installation status to backend

INTEROPERABILITY TEST PLAN:

TEST 1: PACKAGE DOWNLOAD
- Configure package in backend
- Initiate download from vehicle
- Verify package received correctly
- Result: PASS

TEST 2: PACKAGE AUTHENTICATION
- Create signed package
- Verify signature on vehicle
- Attempt unsigned package (should fail)
- Result: PASS

TEST 3: STATUS REPORTING
- Execute update on vehicle
- Verify status reported to backend
- Verify status accurate
- Result: PASS

TEST 4: ERROR HANDLING
- Simulate network interruption
- Verify vehicle handles gracefully
- Verify recovery works
- Result: PASS (after fix applied)

INTEROPERABILITY CONFIRMATION:
Confirmed by: [Integration Lead]
Date: [Date]

SECTION 5.3.4: INTEGRITY
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"5.3.4.1 SHALL: Organization shall establish, implement and maintain
processes to preserve integrity of software, metadata or software update
packages or both during distribution in context of interoperability within
organizations, between organizations in supply chain, from organizations
to vehicles, and within/between vehicle systems.

Notes: Risk-based approaches in ISO/SAE 21434 and ISO 26262 series can
be used to select measures."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

Integrity preservation ensures that software, metadata, and packages are
not modified without authorization during distribution. This is a
fundamental security requirement for software update engineering.

WHY INTEGRITY MATTERS:

1. SAFETY: Modified software could cause safety-critical failures
2. SECURITY: Modified software could introduce vulnerabilities
3. QUALITY: Modified software may not function correctly
4. COMPLIANCE: Modified software may violate regulations
5. LIABILITY: Unauthorized modifications create liability exposure

INTEGRITY THREATS:

Threats to integrity during distribution include:

1. MAN-IN-THE-MIDDLE ATTACKS: Attacker intercepts and modifies packages
   in transit

2. REPOSITORY COMPROMISE: Attacker modifies packages in storage

3. INSIDER THREATS: Authorized personnel make unauthorized modifications

4. SUPPLY CHAIN ATTACKS: Attacker modifies packages at supplier

5. VEHICLE-SIDE ATTACKS: Attacker modifies packages on vehicle

INTEGRITY DOMAINS:

The requirement specifies four integrity domains:

1. WITHIN ORGANIZATIONS:
   - During internal development
   - During internal storage
   - During internal transfer between systems

2. BETWEEN ORGANIZATIONS:
   - During transfer to suppliers
   - During transfer from suppliers
   - During transfer to service providers

3. FROM ORGANIZATIONS TO VEHICLES:
   - During OTA distribution
   - During workshop distribution
   - During USB distribution

4. WITHIN/BETWEEN VEHICLE SYSTEMS:
   - During transfer between ECUs
   - During transfer to target ECU
   - During storage on vehicle

INTEGRITY PRESERVATION MEASURES:

Common integrity preservation measures include:

1. CRYPTOGRAPHIC SIGNATURES:
   - Digital signatures authenticate package source
   - Signature verification detects modifications
   - Signatures should use strong algorithms
   - Private keys must be protected

2. HASH VALUES:
   - Hash values provide integrity checksum
   - Hash verification detects modifications
   - Secure hash algorithms required
   - Hashes must be protected from modification

3. SECURE COMMUNICATION:
   - Encrypted channels protect data in transit
   - TLS/SSL for network communication
   - Secure protocols for vehicle communication

4. ACCESS CONTROLS:
   - Restrict who can modify packages
   - Audit all modifications
   - Detect unauthorized access

5. SECURE STORAGE:
   - Protect packages in storage
   - Detect unauthorized modifications
   - Maintain access controls

RISK-BASED APPROACH:

The standard notes that risk-based approaches from ISO/SAE 21434 and
ISO 26262 can be used to select measures. This means:

1. ASSESS RISK: What is the likelihood and impact of integrity failure?

2. SELECT MEASURES: Choose measures appropriate to risk level

3. IMPLEMENT MEASURES: Apply selected measures effectively

4. VERIFY EFFECTIVENESS: Confirm measures work as intended

Higher-risk situations require stronger measures:

LOW RISK: Simple hash verification may suffice
MEDIUM RISK: Cryptographic signatures recommended
HIGH RISK: Multiple measures, hardware security modules

INTEGRITY PROCESS EXAMPLE:

PROCESS: Software Update Package Integrity Preservation

SCOPE: All software update packages for [Organization]

MEASURES:

1. PACKAGE CREATION:
   - Sign package with production signing key
   - Key stored in Hardware Security Module
   - Signing process in controlled environment
   - Multiple approvals required for signing

2. PACKAGE STORAGE:
   - Store in access-controlled repository
   - Log all access and modifications
   - Maintain hash values for verification
   - Regular integrity checks

3. PACKAGE DISTRIBUTION:
   - Distribute via TLS-encrypted channels
   - Include signature with package
   - Verify signature on receipt
   - Verify hash after download

4. PACKAGE ON VEHICLE:
   - Verify signature before installation
   - Verify hash before installation
   - Store in protected area
   - Verify before activation

VERIFICATION:
- Regular audits of integrity measures
- Penetration testing of distribution systems
- Monitoring for integrity violations
- Incident response for integrity issues

SECTION 6.1: OVERVIEW AND OBJECTIVES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"6.1 OBJECTIVES
Ensure the following are developed:
a) Management of cybersecurity risks for the infrastructure
b) Functionality for collecting and managing vehicle configuration information
c) Functionality for collecting and distributing information about software
   update campaigns
d) Functionality for creating, managing, and distributing software update
   packages"

DETAILED EXPLANATION OF OBJECTIVES:

Clause 6 addresses infrastructure-level requirements for software update
engineering. Infrastructure encompasses the backend systems, processes,
and functions that support software update campaigns. This clause focuses
on developing the capabilities that enable organizations to plan, execute,
and monitor software update campaigns.

OBJECTIVE A: CYBERSECURITY RISK MANAGEMENT FOR INFRASTRUCTURE

Infrastructure for software update engineering represents a critical attack
surface. Backend systems store and distribute software update packages,
manage vehicle configuration information, and communicate with vehicles.
Compromise of these systems could enable distribution of malicious software
to thousands or millions of vehicles simultaneously.

Objective (a) requires that organizations develop cybersecurity risk
management capabilities specifically for the infrastructure supporting
software update engineering. This includes:

1. RISK IDENTIFICATION:
   - What assets exist in the infrastructure?
   - What threats could target the infrastructure?
   - What vulnerabilities might exist?
   - What attack scenarios are possible?

2. RISK ASSESSMENT:
   - How likely are identified attacks?
   - What would be the impact of successful attacks?
   - What is the risk level for each scenario?

3. RISK TREATMENT:
   - What security controls should be implemented?
   - How should residual risk be managed?
   - What monitoring should be established?

4. RISK MONITORING:
   - How will the risk landscape be monitored?
   - How will new threats be identified?
   - How will control effectiveness be measured?

OBJECTIVE B: VEHICLE CONFIGURATION INFORMATION MANAGEMENT

Vehicle configuration information is essential for targeting software updates
correctly. A vehicle's configuration includes hardware variants, software
versions, installed features, calibration data, and market-specific settings.
This information must be collected, stored, and made available to enable:

1. TARGET IDENTIFICATION: Identifying which vehicles should receive updates
2. COMPATIBILITY CHECKING: Verifying updates are compatible with vehicle
3. DEPENDENCY MANAGEMENT: Ensuring required dependencies are satisfied
4. RECALL MANAGEMENT: Identifying vehicles affected by safety issues

Objective (b) requires functionality for:

1. COLLECTING vehicle configuration information from various sources
2. MANAGING this information throughout its lifecycle
3. PROVIDING ACCESS to authorized parties
4. MAINTAINING INTEGRITY of the information

OBJECTIVE C: CAMPAIGN INFORMATION MANAGEMENT

Software update campaigns generate and require significant information flow.
Campaign information includes notifications to vehicle users, status reports
from vehicles, results of update operations, and documentation of campaign
activities.

Objective (c) requires functionality for:

1. COLLECTING campaign information from various sources
2. DISTRIBUTING campaign information to relevant parties
3. STORING campaign information for documentation and analysis
4. REPORTING campaign status and results

OBJECTIVE D: SOFTWARE UPDATE PACKAGE PROCESSING

Software update packages are the core deliverables of software update
engineering. The infrastructure must support the complete lifecycle of
these packages, from creation through distribution.

Objective (d) requires functionality for:

1. CREATING software update packages
2. MANAGING packages in repositories
3. DISTRIBUTING packages to vehicles
4. TRACKING package deployment status

SECTION 6.2: GENERAL REQUIREMENTS
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"6.2 GENERAL
This clause includes requirements for development of infrastructure used for
software update campaigns. Requirements cover functions assigned to
infrastructure for campaigns, such as distribution, communication, information
storage, and cybersecurity. These functions support software update campaigns
in infrastructure, on/off vehicle depending on architectural decisions of
organization."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

Clause 6 establishes requirements for infrastructure development. The term
"development" is significant - organizations must actively develop and
maintain infrastructure capabilities, not just acquire generic IT systems.

INFRASTRUCTURE FUNCTIONS:

The standard identifies key functions that infrastructure must support:

1. DISTRIBUTION:
   - Storing update packages securely
   - Distributing packages to vehicles or intermediaries
   - Managing distribution schedules
   - Tracking distribution status

2. COMMUNICATION:
   - Communicating with vehicles
   - Communicating with vehicle users
   - Communicating with service providers
   - Communicating with regulatory authorities

3. INFORMATION STORAGE:
   - Storing vehicle configuration information
   - Storing package metadata
   - Storing campaign records
   - Storing documentation

4. CYBERSECURITY:
   - Protecting infrastructure assets
   - Securing communications
   - Managing access and authentication
   - Monitoring for security incidents

ARCHITECTURAL FLEXIBILITY:

The standard notes that functions can be located "on/off vehicle depending on
architectural decisions of organization." This provides flexibility:

1. INFRASTRUCTURE-CENTRIC: Most functions in backend systems
   - Vehicles are relatively passive
   - Backend controls campaign execution
   - Requires reliable connectivity

2. VEHICLE-CENTRIC: More functions on vehicle
   - Vehicles have more autonomy
   - Vehicle can execute updates independently
   - Less dependent on connectivity

3. HYBRID: Functions distributed between infrastructure and vehicle
   - Common in current implementations
   - Balances autonomy with coordination
   - Most flexible approach

ARCHITECTURAL DECISION FACTORS:

Organizations make architectural decisions based on:

1. CONNECTIVITY: What connectivity is available to vehicles?
2. VEHICLE CAPABILITY: What processing capability do vehicles have?
3. LATENCY REQUIREMENTS: How quickly must updates be processed?
4. SECURITY REQUIREMENTS: What security measures are needed?
5. COST: What is the cost of different architectures?
6. REGULATORY: What do regulations require or allow?

SECTION 6.3.1: MANAGING RISK
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"6.3.1.1 SHALL: Organization shall manage cybersecurity risks of infrastructure.

Examples: Application of ISO/IEC 27000 series, Application of ISO/SAE 21434
for cybersecurity risks in vehicle."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

Infrastructure cybersecurity risk management is a fundamental requirement.
The standard references ISO/IEC 27000 series and ISO/SAE 21434 as guidance
for implementing this requirement.

INFRASTRUCTURE ASSETS REQUIRING PROTECTION:

1. SOFTWARE UPDATE PACKAGES:
   - Integrity: Packages must not be modified
   - Authenticity: Packages must be from authorized source
   - Availability: Packages must be accessible when needed
   - Confidentiality: Some packages may contain proprietary information

2. VEHICLE CONFIGURATION DATA:
   - Integrity: Configuration must be accurate
   - Confidentiality: Configuration may reveal vehicle capabilities
   - Availability: Configuration needed for campaign execution

3. CRYPTOGRAPHIC KEYS:
   - Private keys for package signing
   - Keys for secure communication
   - Keys for authentication
   - Root certificates

4. INFRASTRUCTURE SYSTEMS:
   - Campaign management systems
   - Package repositories
   - Communication gateways
   - Monitoring systems

5. CUSTOMER DATA:
   - Vehicle owner information
   - Vehicle location data
   - Contact information

THREAT SCENARIOS FOR INFRASTRUCTURE:

1. PACKAGE TAMPERING:
   Threat: Attacker modifies update packages to inject malicious code
   Impact: Malicious software distributed to vehicles
   Likelihood: Medium (requires infrastructure access)
   
2. UNAUTHORIZED PACKAGE DISTRIBUTION:
   Threat: Attacker distributes unauthorized packages to vehicles
   Impact: Vehicles receive unauthorized software
   Likelihood: Medium (requires authentication bypass)

3. DATA EXFILTRATION:
   Threat: Attacker extracts vehicle configuration or customer data
   Impact: Privacy violation, competitive exposure
   Likelihood: Medium to High (valuable target)

4. DENIAL OF SERVICE:
   Threat: Attacker disrupts infrastructure availability
   Impact: Updates cannot be distributed
   Likelihood: Medium (common attack type)

5. KEY COMPROMISE:
   Threat: Attacker obtains private signing keys
   Impact: Can sign malicious packages
   Likelihood: Low (requires significant access)

RISK TREATMENT MEASURES:

1. ACCESS CONTROLS:
   - Multi-factor authentication for infrastructure access
   - Role-based access control
   - Privileged access management
   - Regular access reviews

2. NETWORK SECURITY:
   - Firewalls and network segmentation
   - Intrusion detection/prevention
   - DDoS protection
   - Secure communication channels

3. DATA PROTECTION:
   - Encryption for data at rest
   - Encryption for data in transit
   - Data loss prevention
   - Backup and recovery

4. MONITORING:
   - Security information and event management (SIEM)
   - Log analysis and alerting
   - Anomaly detection
   - Incident response procedures

5. VULNERABILITY MANAGEMENT:
   - Regular vulnerability scanning
   - Patch management
   - Penetration testing
   - Security assessments

IMPLEMENTATION EXAMPLE - INFRASTRUCTURE CYBERSECURITY:

SECURITY ARCHITECTURE FOR OTA INFRASTRUCTURE:

1. NETWORK ARCHITECTURE:
   - DMZ for external-facing services
   - Separate network segments for:
     - Package repositories
     - Campaign management
     - Vehicle communication
     - Administrative access
   - Firewall rules controlling traffic between segments

2. ACCESS CONTROL:
   - Centralized identity management
   - Multi-factor authentication required for:
     - Administrative access
     - Package signing operations
     - Campaign approval
   - Role-based access with principle of least privilege
   - Regular access certification

3. CRYPTOGRAPHIC CONTROLS:
   - Hardware security modules for key storage
   - Production signing keys never leave HSM
   - TLS 1.3 for all communications
   - Certificate pinning for vehicle communication
   - Regular key rotation

4. MONITORING:
   - SIEM collecting logs from all infrastructure
   - Real-time alerting for security events
   - Anomaly detection for suspicious patterns
   - 24/7 security operations center

5. INCIDENT RESPONSE:
   - Documented incident response procedures
   - Defined escalation paths
   - Communication templates
   - Regular incident response exercises

SECTION 6.3.2: MANAGING VEHICLE CONFIGURATION INFORMATION
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"6.3.2.1 SHALL: Infrastructure shall have one or more functions for receiving,
storing and processing of vehicle configuration information.

6.3.2.2 SHALL: Infrastructure shall have one or more functions to maintain
integrity of collected vehicle configuration information.

6.3.2.3 SHALL: Infrastructure shall have one or more functions to distribute
vehicle configuration information to related parties.

Notes: Distribution functions can be manual (paper-based). Vehicle configuration
information can be distributed before, during or after campaign.

6.3.2.4 SHALL: Infrastructure shall have one or more functions to support
identification of dependencies for software update packages.

Notes: Can be done on vehicle, infrastructure or combination. Used in Clause 8.

6.3.2.5 SHALL: Infrastructure shall have one or more functions to check
compatibility of a software update package."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

These requirements establish comprehensive vehicle configuration information
management capability in the infrastructure.

REQUIREMENT 6.3.2.1 - RECEIVE, STORE, AND PROCESS:

Infrastructure must handle vehicle configuration information throughout its
lifecycle:

1. RECEIVING FUNCTIONS:
   - Accept configuration data from manufacturing systems
   - Accept configuration updates from vehicles
   - Accept configuration data from service operations
   - Validate incoming data

2. STORING FUNCTIONS:
   - Store configuration data securely
   - Maintain configuration history
   - Index for efficient retrieval
   - Backup and recovery

3. PROCESSING FUNCTIONS:
   - Analyze configuration data
   - Generate reports and queries
   - Support target identification
   - Support compatibility checking

VEHICLE CONFIGURATION INFORMATION CONTENTS:

1. VEHICLE IDENTIFICATION:
   - VIN (Vehicle Identification Number)
   - Model and model year
   - Manufacturing date and plant
   - Market/region

2. HARDWARE CONFIGURATION:
   - Installed ECUs with part numbers
   - Hardware revisions
   - Installed options and features
   - Sensor and actuator variants

3. SOFTWARE CONFIGURATION:
   - Software versions by ECU
   - Calibration versions
   - Configuration parameters
   - Feature enablement flags

4. SERVICE HISTORY:
   - Previous updates applied
   - Service visits
   - Hardware replacements
   - Configuration changes

REQUIREMENT 6.3.2.2 - INTEGRITY MAINTENANCE:

Configuration data integrity is critical because incorrect configuration
can lead to:

- Incompatible updates being applied
- Updates being sent to wrong vehicles
- Safety or security issues

Integrity measures include:

1. VALIDATION:
   - Validate data format and content
   - Cross-check against known constraints
   - Verify consistency between data elements

2. PROTECTION:
   - Access controls on configuration data
   - Audit logging of changes
   - Change authorization requirements

3. VERIFICATION:
   - Periodic verification against source systems
   - Reconciliation with manufacturing records
   - Vehicle self-reporting verification

REQUIREMENT 6.3.2.3 - DISTRIBUTION:

Configuration information must be distributed to support campaign execution:

1. TO CAMPAIGN MANAGEMENT:
   - Enable target identification
   - Support recipient resolution
   - Provide compatibility data

2. TO SERVICE OPERATIONS:
   - Enable service technicians to understand vehicle state
   - Support diagnostic activities
   - Enable proper update application

3. TO REGULATORY AUTHORITIES:
   - Support recall activities
   - Enable type approval verification
   - Provide compliance evidence

REQUIREMENT 6.3.2.4 - DEPENDENCY IDENTIFICATION:

Dependencies between software components must be identified to enable:

SECTION 7.1: OVERVIEW AND OBJECTIVES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"7.1 OBJECTIVES
Establish the following functionalities in and for vehicles or vehicle systems:
a) Managing safety and cybersecurity risks for software update operations
b) Managing vehicle configuration information
c) Communicating software update campaign information
d) Enabling software update operations, verifying software update packages,
   and managing failures during campaigns"

DETAILED EXPLANATION OF OBJECTIVES:

Clause 7 addresses vehicle-level requirements for software update engineering.
While Clause 6 focused on infrastructure (backend systems), Clause 7 focuses
on the vehicle itself - the functions and capabilities that must exist within
vehicles to support software update operations.

OBJECTIVE A: SAFETY AND CYBERSECURITY RISK MANAGEMENT

Software update operations in vehicles involve inherent risks. An update
could fail, leaving a vehicle in an unsafe state. An update could introduce
vulnerabilities. The update process itself could be exploited by attackers.

Objective (a) requires that vehicles have capabilities to manage these risks:

1. FUNCTIONAL SAFETY RISK MANAGEMENT:
   - Ensure vehicle remains safe during update operations
   - Handle update failures without creating hazards
   - Maintain safety function availability

2. CYBERSECURITY RISK MANAGEMENT:
   - Protect update mechanisms from exploitation
   - Verify authenticity and integrity of updates
   - Prevent unauthorized updates

3. SAFETY OF INTENDED FUNCTION:
   - Ensure update process itself is safe
   - Prevent foreseeable misuse of update capability
   - Maintain safe vehicle state throughout

OBJECTIVE B: VEHICLE CONFIGURATION INFORMATION MANAGEMENT

Vehicles must be able to report and manage their own configuration information.
This is essential for:

1. COMPATIBILITY CHECKING: Vehicle can verify update compatibility
2. TARGET IDENTIFICATION: Vehicle can report its configuration to backend
3. DEPENDENCY MANAGEMENT: Vehicle can identify dependencies
4. STATE TRACKING: Vehicle configuration reflects current state

OBJECTIVE C: CAMPAIGN COMMUNICATION

Vehicles must be able to communicate about software update campaigns:

1. RECEIVE NOTIFICATIONS: Accept campaign notifications
2. REPORT STATUS: Report update status to backend
3. USER COMMUNICATION: Communicate with vehicle user about updates

OBJECTIVE D: UPDATE OPERATION SUPPORT

Vehicles must have the capability to execute update operations:

1. PACKAGE RECEIPT: Receive update packages
2. PACKAGE VERIFICATION: Verify package authenticity and integrity
3. INSTALLATION: Install update to target systems
4. ACTIVATION: Activate new software
5. FAILURE MANAGEMENT: Handle failures during update

SECTION 7.3.1: MANAGING RISKS
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"7.3.1.1 SHALL: Functional safety risks of software update operations in
vehicle shall be managed.

Note 1: Management includes identification, analysis, evaluation and treatment
of risks.
Note 2: ISO 26262 series provides guidance on achieving functional safety
through appropriate requirements and processes.

7.3.1.2 SHALL: Safety risks due to reasonable and foreseeable misuse of
software update operations in vehicle shall be managed.

Note 1: Management includes identification, analysis, evaluation and treatment
of risks.
Note 2: ISO 21448 provides guidance on achieving safety of intended
functionality through appropriate requirements and processes.

7.3.1.3 SHALL: Cybersecurity risks of software update operations in vehicle
shall be managed."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

REQUIREMENT 7.3.1.1 - FUNCTIONAL SAFETY RISK MANAGEMENT:

Functional safety risks arise from potential malfunctions during software
update operations. The vehicle must manage these risks to prevent hazardous
situations.

POTENTIAL FUNCTIONAL SAFETY RISKS:

1. PARTIAL UPDATE FAILURE:
   - Software is only partially written
   - ECU is in undefined state
   - Potential for incorrect behavior

2. POWER LOSS DURING UPDATE:
   - Update interrupted by power loss
   - ECU may be non-functional
   - Vehicle may be stranded

3. INCOMPATIBLE SOFTWARE:
   - Incompatible software installed
   - Safety functions may not work correctly
   - Hazardous behavior possible

4. COMMUNICATION FAILURE:
   - Update interrupted by communication failure
   - Package may be incomplete
   - Installation may be partial

5. ROLLBACK FAILURE:
   - Attempt to rollback fails
   - Vehicle in undefined state
   - Safety functions unavailable

RISK MANAGEMENT APPROACH:

1. RISK IDENTIFICATION:
   - Identify all potential failure scenarios
   - Analyze failure causes and effects
   - Determine severity of potential harm

2. RISK ANALYSIS:
   - Assess probability of failure scenarios
   - Assess controllability by driver
   - Determine ASIL requirements

3. RISK EVALUATION:
   - Compare assessed risk to acceptance criteria
   - Determine if risk reduction is required
   - Prioritize risk treatment

4. RISK TREATMENT:
   - Implement safety measures
   - Design for fault tolerance
   - Implement fail-safe behaviors

SAFETY MEASURES FOR UPDATE OPERATIONS:

1. SAFE STATE MANAGEMENT:
   - Ensure vehicle is in safe state before update
   - Monitor vehicle state during update
   - Maintain safe state throughout

2. POWER MANAGEMENT:
   - Monitor power supply during update
   - Abort update if power is insufficient
   - Maintain rollback capability

3. ROLLBACK CAPABILITY:
   - Preserve previous software version
   - Enable rollback on failure
   - Verify rollback success

4. UPDATE VALIDATION:
   - Verify update integrity before installation
   - Verify compatibility before activation
   - Validate functionality after activation

REQUIREMENT 7.3.1.2 - MISUSE RISK MANAGEMENT:

Safety risks from foreseeable misuse must be managed. Foreseeable misuse
includes:

1. USER INITIATES UPDATE WHILE DRIVING:
   - Risk: Vehicle distracted during critical operation
   - Mitigation: Require vehicle to be parked

2. USER INTERRUPTS UPDATE PROCESS:
   - Risk: Partial update, ECU in undefined state
   - Mitigation: Prevent user interruption, or ensure safe abort

3. USER IGNORES UPDATE NOTIFICATIONS:
   - Risk: Critical safety update not applied
   - Mitigation: Persistent notification, forced update for critical cases

4. USER APPLIES UPDATE INCORRECTLY:
   - Risk: Update applied under wrong conditions
   - Mitigation: Clear instructions, automated verification

MISUSE RISK MANAGEMENT APPROACH:

1. IDENTIFY FORESEEABLE MISUSE:
   - Analyze how users might misuse update capability
   - Consider different user types and capabilities
   - Consider different situations and contexts

2. ASSESS MISUSE CONSEQUENCES:
   - Determine what could go wrong
   - Assess severity of potential harm
   - Assess likelihood of misuse

3. IMPLEMENT MITIGATIONS:
   - Design to prevent misuse
   - Design to detect misuse
   - Design to mitigate consequences

REQUIREMENT 7.3.1.3 - CYBERSECURITY RISK MANAGEMENT:

Cybersecurity risks of update operations must be managed. Potential threats
include:

1. UNAUTHORIZED UPDATE:
   - Threat: Attacker installs unauthorized software
   - Impact: Malicious code on vehicle
   - Mitigation: Strong authentication, signed packages

2. PACKAGE TAMPERING:
   - Threat: Attacker modifies package in transit
   - Impact: Malicious code installed
   - Mitigation: Integrity verification, encryption

3. REPLAY ATTACK:
   - Threat: Attacker replays valid package
   - Impact: Older (vulnerable) version installed
   - Mitigation: Version checking, anti-replay

4. DENIAL OF SERVICE:
   - Threat: Attacker prevents update
   - Impact: Critical update not applied
   - Mitigation: Alternative update methods

5. MANIPULATION OF UPDATE STATE:
   - Threat: Attacker manipulates update state
   - Impact: Update bypasses verification
   - Mitigation: Secure state management

CYBERSECURITY RISK MANAGEMENT APPROACH:

1. THREAT ANALYSIS:
   - Identify threat actors and capabilities
   - Identify attack vectors
   - Identify assets to protect

2. RISK ASSESSMENT:
   - Assess attack feasibility
   - Assess impact of successful attack
   - Determine risk level

3. SECURITY REQUIREMENTS:
   - Define security requirements for update system
   - Allocate requirements to components
   - Define verification criteria

4. SECURITY IMPLEMENTATION:
   - Implement security controls
   - Implement secure boot
   - Implement secure storage
   - Implement secure communication

7.3.4.5 SHALL: There shall be one or more functions to handle interruptions
in communications while receiving a software update package.

7.3.4.6 SHALL: There shall be one or more functions to verify integrity and
authenticity of received software update package any time before activation.

Note: Can be implemented either in vehicle or infrastructure or both.

7.3.4.7 SHALL: There shall be one or more functions to maintain integrity of
software update packages and their contents:
- From infrastructure to vehicles
- Within vehicle and between vehicle systems

7.3.4.8 SHALL: There shall be one or more functions to check compatibility
of software update package before activation.

Note: Can be implemented either in vehicle or infrastructure or both.

7.3.4.9 SHALL: There shall be one or more functions to ensure safe vehicle
state in each step of software update operation.

7.3.4.10 SHALL: There shall be one or more functions to ensure vehicle safety
if software update operation fails."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.1 - DISTRIBUTION METHOD SUPPORT:

Vehicles must support applicable distribution methods:

1. OTA METHODS:
   - Cellular reception and processing
   - Wi-Fi reception and processing
   - Resume interrupted downloads

2. DIAGNOSTIC METHODS:
   - Diagnostic interface support
   - UDS protocol support
   - Transfer via diagnostic tool

3. USB METHODS:
   - USB interface support
   - Package reading from USB
   - USB file system handling

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.2 - UPDATE OPERATION SUPPORT:

Vehicles must support the complete update operation:

1. RECEIPT:
   - Receive package from distribution channel
   - Store package temporarily
   - Verify receipt completeness

2. INSTALLATION:
   - Extract package components
   - Program target ECU(s)
   - Verify installation success

3. ACTIVATION:
   - Switch to new software
   - Initialize new software
   - Verify activation success

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.3 - CONDITION VERIFICATION:

The vehicle must verify conditions before proceeding with updates:

1. VEHICLE STATE CONDITIONS:
   - Vehicle is stationary
   - Transmission in park
   - Parking brake engaged

2. POWER CONDITIONS:
   - Battery sufficient
   - Power stable
   - No cranking during update

3. RESOURCE CONDITIONS:
   - Storage available
   - Memory available
   - Network available (if needed)

4. ENVIRONMENTAL CONDITIONS:
   - Temperature within range
   - No active safety-critical operations

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.4 - ACCESS ARBITRATION:

Multiple parties may try to access the vehicle simultaneously:

1. POTENTIAL CONFLICTS:
   - OTA update request while diagnostic session active
   - Multiple update campaigns targeting same vehicle
   - User operation while update in progress

2. ARBITRATION RULES:
   - Safety-critical operations have priority
   - Active diagnostic sessions may block OTA
   - User-initiated operations may have priority

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.5 - COMMUNICATION INTERRUPTION HANDLING:

Network interruptions during download must be handled:

1. DETECTION:
   - Detect connection loss
   - Detect data interruption
   - Detect timeout conditions

2. HANDLING:
   - Pause download
   - Preserve partial download
   - Resume when connection restored
   - Restart if resume not possible

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.6 - INTEGRITY AND AUTHENTICITY VERIFICATION:

Packages must be verified before activation:

1. INTEGRITY VERIFICATION:
   - Verify package hash
   - Verify package signature
   - Detect any modifications

2. AUTHENTICITY VERIFICATION:
   - Verify signature against trusted certificates
   - Verify package source
   - Detect unauthorized packages

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.7 - IN-PACKAGE INTEGRITY:

Package integrity must be maintained throughout:

1. INFRASTRUCTURE TO VEHICLE:
   - Secure transfer protocols
   - End-to-end integrity verification

2. WITHIN VEHICLE:
   - Secure storage on vehicle
   - Integrity during transfer between ECUs
   - Integrity during installation

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.8 - COMPATIBILITY CHECKING:

Compatibility must be verified before activation:

1. HARDWARE COMPATIBILITY:
   - ECU hardware matches package requirements
   - Required sensors/actuators present

2. SOFTWARE COMPATIBILITY:
   - Current software version compatible
   - Dependencies satisfied

3. CONFIGURATION COMPATIBILITY:
   - Vehicle configuration matches requirements
   - Required features present

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.9 - SAFE VEHICLE STATE:

Vehicle must remain safe throughout update:

1. BEFORE UPDATE:
   - Verify safe starting state
   - Disable functions that could compromise safety

2. DURING UPDATE:
   - Monitor vehicle state
   - Prevent vehicle motion
   - Maintain essential safety functions

3. DURING ACTIVATION:
   - Control activation timing
   - Verify successful activation
   - Enable functions only after verification

REQUIREMENT 7.3.4.10 - FAILURE SAFETY:

If update fails, vehicle safety must be ensured:

1. FAILURE DETECTION:
   - Detect installation failure
   - Detect activation failure
   - Detect post-update malfunction

2. FAILURE RESPONSE:
   - Automatic rollback if possible
   - Safe degraded mode
   - User notification
   - Safe vehicle state maintenance

SECTION 8.1: OVERVIEW AND OBJECTIVES
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"8.1 OBJECTIVES
Ensure the following are performed:
a) Identifying the target(s) and contents of software update package
b) Assembling software update package containing necessary software and metadata
c) Verifying and validating software update package
d) Approving release of software update package"

DETAILED EXPLANATION OF OBJECTIVES:

Clause 8 addresses software update package development. A software update
package is the deliverable unit that contains all the software and metadata
needed to perform an update. This clause covers the complete package
lifecycle from identification through approval.

OBJECTIVE A: TARGET AND CONTENT IDENTIFICATION

Before a package can be assembled, the organization must identify:

1. WHAT TO UPDATE: Which vehicles, systems, or ECUs are targets?
2. WHAT TO INSTALL: What software and data should be included?
3. WHAT CONSTRAINTS: What compatibility, dependency, and condition constraints
   apply?

This identification ensures that packages are properly scoped and contain
the correct elements for their intended targets.

OBJECTIVE B: PACKAGE ASSEMBLY

Once targets and contents are identified, the package must be assembled:

1. SOFTWARE SELECTION: Select the correct software versions
2. METADATA CREATION: Create comprehensive metadata
3. PACKAGE CREATION: Package everything together
4. IDENTIFICATION: Assign unique package identifier

OBJECTIVE C: VERIFICATION AND VALIDATION

Packages must be verified and validated before release:

1. VERIFICATION: Does the package contain what was intended?
2. VALIDATION: Does the package work correctly on target systems?

OBJECTIVE D: APPROVAL FOR RELEASE

Packages must be formally approved before distribution:

1. APPROVAL CRITERIA: What must be satisfied for approval?
2. APPROVAL AUTHORITY: Who can approve packages?
3. APPROVAL DOCUMENTATION: How is approval documented?

for each software update campaign.

9.3.1.14 SHALL: Organization shall determine necessary information to be
communicated concerning each software update campaign including related
parties and methods.

9.3.1.15 SHALL: Organization shall create software update campaign plan for
each campaign based on 9.3.1.1 to 9.3.1.14 and relevant parts of Clause 8."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

CAMPAIGN PLANNING REQUIREMENTS:

The preparation phase requires comprehensive planning:

1. PURPOSE (9.3.1.1):
   - Why is this campaign happening?
   - What are the objectives?
   - What outcomes are expected?

2. ORGANIZATION (9.3.1.2):
   - Who is responsible for the campaign?
   - Who makes decisions?
   - Who executes activities?

3. PACKAGE SELECTION (9.3.1.3-4):
   - Which packages are included?
   - Are they approved for release?
   - Are they compatible together?

4. CONFIGURATION IMPACT (9.3.1.5):
   - What configuration changes will occur?
   - What data needs to be updated?
   - How will configuration be tracked?

5. DISTRIBUTION METHOD (9.3.1.6):
   - How will packages be delivered?
   - OTA? Workshop? USB?
   - Multiple methods for different recipients?

6. TARGET DEFINITION (9.3.1.7):
   - Which vehicles are targeted?
   - What criteria define the target population?
   - Are there exclusions?

7. EXECUTION CONDITIONS (9.3.1.8):
   - When can the campaign run?
   - What external conditions are required?
   - What constraints apply?

8. DEPENDENCY HANDLING (9.3.1.9):
   - What dependencies exist between packages?
   - How will dependencies be managed?
   - What sequencing is required?

9. FAILURE RESPONSE (9.3.1.10):
   - What could go wrong?
   - How will failures be handled?
   - What corrective actions are planned?

10. CYBERSECURITY (9.3.1.11):
    - What security measures are needed?
    - How will security be monitored?
    - What incident response is planned?

11. SPECIAL REQUIREMENTS (9.3.1.12):
    - Is special equipment needed?
    - Are skilled persons required?
    - What actions are needed?

12. USER CONFIRMATION (9.3.1.13):
    - Is user confirmation required?
    - How will confirmation be obtained?
    - What if user declines?

13. COMMUNICATION (9.3.1.14):
    - Who needs to be informed?
    - What information do they need?
    - How will they be informed?

14. CAMPAIGN PLAN (9.3.1.15):
    - Compile all planning elements
    - Create documented campaign plan
    - Obtain approval

SECTION 9.3.2: SOFTWARE UPDATE CAMPAIGN EXECUTION
================================================================================

EXACT REQUIREMENT TEXT FROM THE STANDARD:

"9.3.2.1 SHALL: Vehicle configuration information necessary to resolve
targets into recipients shall be obtained for software update campaign.

9.3.2.2 SHALL: Targets of software update campaign shall be resolved into
recipients.

9.3.2.3 SHALL: Processes required in Clause 8 and 9.3.1 shall be completed
before initiating any software update operation during campaign execution.

9.3.2.4 SHALL: Software update package(s) shall be distributed to recipient(s).

9.3.2.5 SHALL: Necessary conditions to perform software update operation
shall be met at latest prior to activation.

Note: Necessary conditions can be met by either vehicle or infrastructure or both.

9.3.2.6 SHALL: Simultaneous access requests during software update operation
shall be managed to maintain safe vehicle state.

9.3.2.7 SHALL: Integrity and authenticity of software update package shall
be verified at latest prior to activation.

Note: Can be performed either in vehicle or infrastructure or both.

9.3.2.8 SHALL: Compatibility for software update package(s) shall be confirmed
at latest prior to activation.

Note: Can be performed either in vehicle or infrastructure or both.

9.3.2.9 SHOULD: Vehicle user should be informed about availability of
software update campaigns affecting their vehicle.

9.3.2.10 SHOULD: Vehicle user should be informed at latest prior to activation
about:
- Purposes of software update campaign
- Criticality of software update campaign
- Instructions for safely performing software update operation
- Changes in vehicle functions due to campaign
- Vehicle functions unavailable during software update and implications for user
- Estimated time vehicle/specific functions will be unavailable

9.3.2.11 SHOULD: Software update campaign should obtain confirmation from
vehicle user at latest prior to activation according to campaign plan.

9.3.2.12 SHALL: Vehicle user shall be notified if required to perform action
during software update campaign execution.

9.3.2.13 SHALL: Software update campaign activities requiring special
equipment or training shall be performed by skilled person.

9.3.2.14 SHALL: Corrective action shall be performed in accordance with
campaign plan when software update operation fails.

9.3.2.15 SHOULD: Status of software update campaign execution for each
recipient should be obtained.

9.3.2.16 SHOULD: Results of software update operation for each recipient
should be reported in appropriate time frame to related parties as determined
by campaign plan.

9.3.2.17 SHALL: Identified information about content of software update
campaign shall be communicated to related parties as documented in campaign
plan, including any changes to user manual."

DETAILED EXPLANATION:

CAMPAIGN EXECUTION PROCESS:

1. PREPARATION COMPLETION (9.3.2.1-3):
   - Get vehicle configuration data
   - Resolve targets to recipients
   - Complete all preparation activities

2. DISTRIBUTION (9.3.2.4):
   - Send packages to vehicles
   - Track distribution status
   - Handle distribution failures

3. PRE-ACTIVATION VERIFICATION (9.3.2.5-8):
   - Verify conditions are met
   - Manage concurrent access
   - Verify package integrity
   - Verify compatibility

4. USER COMMUNICATION (9.3.2.9-12):
   - Inform user of available updates
   - Provide update information
   - Obtain confirmation if needed
   - Notify of required actions

5. EXECUTION CONTROL (9.3.2.13-14):
   - Ensure skilled person present if needed
   - Execute corrective actions on failure

6. MONITORING AND REPORTING (9.3.2.15-17):
   - Track campaign status
   - Report results
   - Communicate campaign content

SECTION 8.2: UN R156 RELATIONSHIP
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

UN Regulation No. 156 establishes requirements for Software Update and
Software Update Management Systems (SUMS). ISO 24089 provides the technical
foundation for compliance with UN R156.

UN R156 KEY REQUIREMENTS:

1. SOFTWARE UPDATE MANAGEMENT SYSTEM:
   - SUMS must be established and maintained
   - Corresponds to ISO 24089 Clause 4 organizational requirements

2. VEHICLE TYPE APPROVAL:
   - Vehicles must have update capability
   - Corresponds to ISO 24089 Clause 7 vehicle requirements

3. UPDATE DOCUMENTATION:
   - Records must be maintained
   - Corresponds to ISO 24089 documentation requirements

4. CYBERSECURITY:
   - Updates must maintain cybersecurity
   - Corresponds to ISO 24089 integration with ISO/SAE 21434

ISO 24089 IMPLEMENTATION FOR UN R156 COMPLIANCE:

- Clause 4 provides SUMS organizational framework
- Clause 5 provides project management requirements
- Clause 6 provides infrastructure requirements
- Clause 7 provides vehicle capability requirements
- Clause 8 provides package development requirements
- Clause 9 provides campaign execution requirements

SECTION 8.4: FUTURE CONTEXT
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SOFTWARE-DEFINED VEHICLES (SDV):

ISO 24089 provides the foundation for software update engineering in the
emerging SDV paradigm:

1. CONTINUOUS UPDATES:
   - SDVs require frequent software updates
   - ISO 24089 provides the framework for managing continuous updates

2. FEATURE ON DEMAND:
   - SDVs enable post-sale feature activation
   - ISO 24089 supports feature enablement through updates

3. SERVICE-ORIENTED ARCHITECTURE:
   - SDVs use service-oriented software
   - ISO 24089 supports service-level updates

EDGE COMPUTING AND V2X:

5G and V2X technologies impact software update engineering:

1. FASTER DISTRIBUTION:
   - 5G enables faster package downloads
   - Larger packages become feasible for OTA

2. VEHICLE-TO-VEHICLE UPDATES:
   - Peer-to-peer update distribution
   - Reduced infrastructure load

3. EDGE PROCESSING:
   - Updates processed at network edge
   - Faster response times

AUTONOMOUS VEHICLES:

Autonomous vehicles present unique update challenges:

1. SAFETY-CRITICAL UPDATES:
   - Autonomous systems are highly safety-critical
   - Rigorous V&V requirements apply

2. SENSOR CALIBRATION:
   - Sensor updates may require recalibration
   - Additional skilled person requirements

3. REGULATORY REQUIREMENTS:
   - Autonomous vehicles face additional regulations
   - Enhanced documentation requirements
